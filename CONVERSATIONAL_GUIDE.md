# Conversational Taglish & Cebuano-English (Bislish) Guide

This guide serves as a reference for generating natural, modern, and colloquial conversational language in the Philippines. It covers **Taglish** (Tagalog + English) which is predominantly spoken in Metro Manila and Luzon, and **Cebuano-English / Bislish** which is predominantly spoken in Visayas and Mindanao.

---

## 1. Taglish (Tagalog + English)

Taglish is not just randomly mixing words; it follows structural patterns. English verbs are often conjugated using Tagalog prefixes, and Tagalog particles are heavily used to convey emotion and tone.

### Key Rules & Patterns

**1. Tagalized English Verbs (Conjugation)**
English verbs are conjugated using Tagalog prefixes (`nag-`, `mag-`, `i-`, `in-`).
* **Past Tense (nag- / in-):** "Nag-email na ako sa HR." / "In-upload ko na yung file."
* **Present Tense (nag- + doubling):** "Nagrereply pa lang siya." / "Naglo-load pa yung website."
* **Future Tense (mag- / i-):** "Magme-message ako mamaya." / "I-send mo na lang sa Viber."

**2. Essential Particles (The "Flavor" of Taglish)**
These small words completely change the tone of a sentence.
* **Ba** (Question marker): "Done ka na *ba*?" (Are you done?)
* **Na** (Already/Now): "Let's go *na*." (Let's go now.)
* **Pa** (Still/Yet): "Wait, loading *pa*." (Wait, it's still loading.)
* **Pala** (Realization/By the way): "Ay, offline *pala* siya." (Oh, I just realized he's offline.)
* **Naman** (Contrast/Please/Moderation): "Send mo *naman* yung link." (Please send the link.) / "Okay *naman* yung design." (The design is actually okay.)
* **Kasi / Kase** (Because): "Error *kasi* yung API." (Because the API had an error.)
* **Daw / Raw** (Reported speech): "Sira *daw* yung server." (They said the server is down.)
* **Kaya** (Speculation/Wondering): "Bakit *kaya* nag-crash?" (I wonder why it crashed?)

**3. Common Corporate/Dev Taglish Slang**
* **"Pa-check naman..."** (Please check...)
* **"Paki-send..."** (Please send...)
* **"As in..."** (Literally / Really)
* **"Bale..."** (So basically... / Therefore...)
* **"Naka-deploy na"** (It is currently deployed)

### Example Sentences (Taglish)
* *Formal:* "Please verify if the deployment is successful."
* *Taglish:* "Pa-check naman kung okay na yung deployment."
* *Formal:* "I will fix the bug tomorrow because I am tired."
* *Taglish:* "Bukas ko na i-fix yung bug, pagod na kasi ako eh."
* *Formal:* "Did you receive the email?"
* *Taglish:* "Na-receive mo ba yung email?"

## 2. Cebuano-English (Bislish / Cebuano Conyo)

Bislish (Bisaya + English) is widely used in Cebu, Davao, and other VisMin areas. Similar to Taglish, it mixes English verbs with Cebuano affixes and uses Cebuano particles. 

### Key Rules & Patterns

**1. Cebuano-lized English Verbs (Conjugation)**
English verbs are conjugated using Cebuano affixes (`ni-`, `mu-`, `naka-`, `gi-`, `pag-`).
* **Past Tense (ni- / gi- / naka-):** "Ni-reply na siya." (He replied.) / "Gi-send na nako." (I sent it.) / "Naka-upload na ko." (I was able to upload.)
* **Present/Progressive (nag-):** "Nag-loading pa." (It's still loading.)
* **Future Tense (mu- / i- / mag-):** "Mu-send ko unya." (I will send later.) / "I-push na ang code." (Push the code now.)
* **Command (pag- / i-):** "Pag-refresh daw." (Try to refresh.)

**2. Essential Particles (The "Flavor" of Bislish)**
* **Ba** (Emphasis/Question): "Sure ka *ba*?" (Are you sure?) / "Lage *ba*." (I know, right.)
* **Na** (Already/Now): "Done *na* ko." (I am done.)
* **Pa** (Still/Yet): "Wait lang, naa *pa* koy gi-fix." (Wait, I am still fixing something.)
* **Diay** (Realization/By the way): "Ay, sayop *diay*." (Oh, it's actually wrong.)
* **Gyud / Jud** (Definitely / Really): "Nindot *jud* ang design." (The design is really nice.) / "Kailangan *jud* nimo i-test." (You really need to test it.)
* **Lage / Lagi** (Agreement / I told you so): "Lage, nakita nako." (Yeah, I saw it.)
* **Kuan** (Filler word like "Uhm" or "Whatchamacallit"): "I-click ra nang *kuan*..." (Just click the uhm...)
* **Daw** (Reported speech / Request): "I-restart *daw* nimo." (They said you should restart it.)
* **Man** (Reasoning/Emphasis): "Ngano *man* nag-error?" (Why did it error?)

**3. Davao Conyo (A specific sub-dialect)**
Davao uses a very specific mix of Tagalog, Bisaya, and English. 
* Uses "gawin" (Tagalog) with Bisaya pronouns: "Gawin nako na later." (I'll do that later.)
* Heavy use of "kasi" instead of "kay": "Mahal kasi gyud ang gas." (Because gas is really expensive.)

### Example Sentences (Bislish)
* *Formal:* "Please verify if the deployment is successful."
* *Bislish:* "Pa-check daw if okay na ang deployment."
* *Formal:* "I will fix the bug tomorrow because I am tired."
* *Bislish:* "Ugma na nako i-fix ang bug, gikapoy na jud ko."
* *Formal:* "Did you receive the email?"
* *Bislish:* "Na-receive nimo ang email?" / "Na-dawat nimo ang email?"
* *Formal:* "I can't open the file."
* *Bislish:* "Dili nako ma-open ang file."

---

## Conclusion
When adopting these conversational tones:
1. **Be natural, not forced.** Don't over-insert English or local words. 
2. **Use particles correctly.** A misplaced "naman" or "jud" sounds robotic.
3. **Keep technical terms in English.** Filipinos rarely translate technical jargon (e.g., "I-deploy", "Nag-crash", "Gi-commit").
