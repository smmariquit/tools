Most people ask how much they can borrow from Pag-IBIG as if there is a single number. There are really two ceilings, and your loan is whichever one is lower. The first is what your income can service every month. The second is how much the property is worth to Pag-IBIG, not to the seller. People plan around the first and get ambushed by the second. The whole program sits under the [Home Development Mutual Fund Law (RA 9679)](https://lawphil.net/statutes/repacts/ra2009/ra_9679_2009.html), and current loan amounts run up to ₱6 million for qualified members.

## Ceiling one: what your income can service

Pag-IBIG sizes your maximum monthly amortization at roughly 35% of your gross monthly income. That percentage is the lever everything else hangs on. Take a ₱50,000 gross income; 35% gives you about ₱17,500 of monthly amortization to work with. At an end-user rate around 6.25% over a 30-year term, ₱17,500 a month supports a loan of roughly ₱2.8 million. Stretch the term shorter or assume a higher repricing rate and that same income supports noticeably less, because the monthly payment per million borrowed climbs.

The honest caveat is that "gross income" on paper is not the same as what survives your existing obligations. If you already carry a car loan or a credit-card balance, the bank processing your Pag-IBIG loan folds those into the affordability test, and your usable 35% shrinks. I have seen approvals come back far below what a clean salary suggested, purely because of an existing motorcycle loan the borrower forgot to mention.

## Ceiling two: the appraisal, not the asking price

Here is the trap that catches first-time buyers. Even if your income qualifies you for ₱2.8 million, Pag-IBIG lends against a percentage of the appraised value of the property, not the seller's asking price. For higher loan amounts that loan-to-value ratio drops, often to around 80% to 90%, which means you must cover the gap in cash as equity. If you found a house listed at ₱3 million but Pag-IBIG appraises it at ₱2.7 million and lends 80% of that, the loan is ₱2.16 million and you bridge the rest, around ₱840,000, out of pocket. Your income ceiling was never the binding constraint; the appraisal was.

This is also why your affordability is not a fixed personal number. It moves with the specific property, because the appraisal moves. The same buyer can be "approved" for very different amounts on two houses priced identically, depending on how each appraises.

### How many contributions do I need before I can apply?

You generally need at least 24 monthly contributions to qualify for a Pag-IBIG housing loan, and you must be an active member with the capacity to pay. A common workaround for a thin record is to pay the difference in a lump sum to reach the required count, but you still have to clear the income and appraisal tests afterward.

### Does a longer term mean I can afford a bigger house?

It lowers your monthly amortization, so within the 35% income ceiling a 30-year term lets you borrow more than a 15-year term for the same monthly cash outlay. The cost is total interest: stretching the term can roughly double the interest you pay over the life of the loan. It buys access, not savings.

Once you have a target loan amount, kudos to Pag-IBIG for publishing its own [online housing-loan affordability calculator](https://www.pagibigfundservices.com/ac/): running your figures through the official tool alongside mine is the cleanest way to catch a discrepancy before you file. If you are buying property often enough to want the rules cold, *Real Estate Exam Reviewer* by Atty. Elvin Villanueva is a book I personally found worth keeping; it is written for licensure candidates but explains loan-to-value, appraisal, and equity in a way ordinary buyers benefit from.
