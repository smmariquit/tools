The moment most examinees miss is that a PRC board exam result is two tests at once. There is the general average you need to reach, and there is a separate per-subject floor you must not fall below, and clearing the first does not save you if you breach the second. People walk out feeling confident because they sense their overall score is past 75, then find a single weak subject pulled the whole result into a conditional or a failure. ## It is a weighted average, set by your profession

The Professional Regulation Commission operates under the [PRC Modernization Act of 2000 (RA 8981)](https://lawphil.net/statutes/repacts/ra2000/ra_8981_2000.html), and each professional board sets the subjects, their weights, and the passing standard for its own exam. Your rating is not the simple average of your subject scores. Each subject carries a percentage weight that reflects how much that board considers it core to practice, so a heavily weighted subject moves your general average far more than a lightly weighted one.

The concrete consequence is that two examinees with the same arithmetic average can land on opposite sides of the line. Suppose your board weights its major subject at 40 percent and you score modestly there but excel in the lighter subjects; your general average can sit lower than a classmate who did the reverse, even though your raw scores look similar. This is why generic advice to study everything equally is wrong. Study toward the weights your specific board publishes, because that is the formula your rating actually uses.

## The disqualifying floor most people forget

For many boards the passing standard is a general average of at least 75 percent with no individual subject rating below a specified floor, commonly 50 percent though it varies by profession. That second clause is the silent failer. You can average above 75 and still not pass if even one subject dips beneath the floor, which converts what looks like a clear pass into a conditional status or an outright failure depending on the board's rules.

Some boards offer a removal or conditional examination in this exact situation, letting you re-sit only the deficient subject within a set window rather than retaking the entire exam, provided your other ratings are strong enough. Whether that lifeline exists, and the cutoffs that trigger it, are specific to your board, so the disqualifying floor and the conditional rules are the two things to confirm in your profession's official guidelines before you assume a near-pass is a pass.

### Is the board exam rating a simple average of my subjects?

No, and assuming it is can mislead you badly. Each subject is weighted by a percentage that your specific PRC board assigns, then those weighted scores are summed into your general average. A subject worth 40 percent of the exam affects your result roughly four times as much as one worth 10 percent, so where you spend your final weeks of review should track those weights.

### Can I pass the average but still fail the exam?

Yes, if your board enforces a minimum per-subject rating and you fall below it in even one subject. Many professions disqualify a score under their published floor regardless of your general average, sometimes routing you to a conditional or removal exam instead of a clean pass. Always check both numbers, the general average and the lowest subject allowed, for your profession.
