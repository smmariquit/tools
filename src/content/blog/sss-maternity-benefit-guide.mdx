The SSS maternity benefit confuses people for one structural reason: the amount is not based on your current salary. It is based on a historical average of your salary credits, picked from a specific window before you gave birth, and that window is defined in a way that almost nobody computes correctly on the first try. The [105-Day Expanded Maternity Leave Law (RA 11210)](https://lawphil.net/statutes/repacts/ra2019/ra_11210_2019.html) governs the entitlement, and the [Social Security Act (RA 11199)](https://lawphil.net/statutes/repacts/ra2019/ra_11199_2019.html) supplies the salary-credit machinery underneath it.

## What you are entitled to, and for how long

A qualified member gets 105 days of paid leave for a live childbirth, regardless of how the delivery happened. A solo parent recognized under the Solo Parents' Welfare Act receives an extra 15 days, bringing it to 120. A miscarriage or emergency termination of pregnancy is covered for 60 days. The law also lets a mother transfer up to 7 of her days to the child's father or an alternate caregiver, and she may extend the leave by an additional 30 days without pay if she chooses.

Eligibility hinges on contributions, not tenure. You need at least three monthly SSS contributions within the 12-month period immediately preceding the semester of your childbirth or miscarriage. The phrase "semester of contingency" is the part that trips everyone: a semester here is two consecutive quarters, and you count the 12 months *before* that semester to find the contributions that matter. Get the semester boundary wrong and you will pull the wrong months into the average.

## How the peso amount is computed

The benefit is built from your Average Monthly Salary Credit. SSS takes the six highest monthly salary credits within that relevant 12-month period, averages them, and that is your AMSC. The daily benefit is the AMSC divided by 180, and the total benefit is that daily figure multiplied by the number of leave days you are entitled to.

Because the Monthly Salary Credit is capped at ₱35,000 under RA 11199, the benefit has a hard ceiling. If all six of your highest credits are at the ₱35,000 maximum, your AMSC is ₱35,000, your daily benefit is ₱35,000 × 6 ÷ 180, which is ₱1,166.67, and a 105-day live-birth benefit tops out at roughly ₱122,500. No matter how high your actual salary climbs, the maternity benefit cannot exceed what the capped salary credit allows, which is the single most common reason a high earner feels the payout looks low.

### Does my employer still pay me my full salary while I am on leave?

This is the detail employers most often miss. RA 11210 introduced the salary differential: for private-sector employees, the employer must pay the difference between the full pay the employee would have received and the SSS maternity benefit, so that the employee effectively receives her full salary during the leave. The SSS benefit alone, capped by the salary credit, is frequently less than full pay, and the employer is on the hook for the gap unless they qualify for one of the narrow exemptions (such as a distressed establishment or a micro-business). For SSS members, the employer also advances the SSS benefit first and is later reimbursed by SSS.

### What if I am self-employed or a voluntary member?

You are still entitled, as long as you meet the same three-contributions-in-the-relevant-12-months rule. The difference is that there is no employer to advance the money or to pay a salary differential, so you claim the benefit directly from SSS and receive only the computed amount based on your own posted contributions. This is exactly why voluntary members benefit from keeping their salary credits high in the months before a planned pregnancy, since those are the figures the six-highest average will draw from.
