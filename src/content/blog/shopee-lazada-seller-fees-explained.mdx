The first payout is where the optimism dies. You sold a ₱1,000 item, the order went through, and the amount that hit your seller wallet was ₱750. Nothing went wrong; that is simply how marketplace economics work, and the ₱250 you are missing is not a single fee but a stack of them charged at different points. If you do not model the full stack before you list a product, you can run a busy store that loses money on every sale. Rates shift often as the platforms adjust their programs, so treat the figures below as the current shape of things rather than permanent law, and confirm against your own seller dashboard before you price.

## The two fees you always pay

Every sale carries a transaction fee and a commission fee, and they are charged separately. The transaction fee, around 2.24 percent inclusive of VAT on Shopee and Lazada, covers the payment rails: the credit card, the GCash, the cash-on-delivery handling. The quiet detail that catches sellers is the base it applies to. The transaction fee is computed on the total order amount including the shipping the buyer paid, not just your item price. If a buyer pays ₱1,000 for the product and ₱100 for shipping, the 2.24 percent bites on the full ₱1,100. The commission fee is the platform's cut for the marketplace itself, generally in the 3 to 5 percent range and varying by product category, with Mall or official-store tiers usually charged more than regular sellers.

## The opt-in programs that quietly stack on top

The free-shipping and cashback badges your competitors wear are not free; they are paid promotional programs, and the cost is a percentage of your item price layered on top of the base fees. Shopee's Free Shipping program adds roughly 5 to 6 percent, and its coin cashback program adds another 3 percent or so. They feel optional until you notice that products without the badges get buried in search, which is the gentle coercion that makes most sellers opt in anyway. TikTok Shop's affiliate model is the heaviest version of this: letting creators sell for you can mean handing over 10 to 15 percent of the sale as commission to the creator, on top of the platform's own fee. Join shipping and cashback and lean on affiliates, and you can be giving up 15 percent or more of gross revenue before the base commission and transaction fees are even counted.

## The newer cut: the BIR's 1 percent withholding

Since 2024 the platforms are required to withhold a creditable withholding tax, currently 1 percent, on a portion of the gross remittances they send sellers, and it shows up as another line shrinking your payout. It is not a new tax, it is an advance against the income tax you owe anyway, and it is creditable when you file. The practical effect for a small seller is cashflow: you receive slightly less now and reconcile it later, which only stings if you were not tracking it. Sellers below the registration thresholds are generally meant to be exempt, but the cleaner move is to register and treat the withheld amount as a prepayment rather than a loss. The rules here are still settling, so check the current treatment before assuming it applies to you.

<RegionalNote>
The platform and the BIR are not the last hands out. If you register your shop as a business — which crossing the BIR thresholds effectively forces — your **LGU** also collects an annual **Local Business Tax / mayor's permit**, graduated on gross receipts at a rate each city sets, and renewed every January. It is easy to overlook because it is billed once a year rather than per order, but it belongs in your margin math alongside the per-sale fees. Check your own city hall's business-tax schedule, since the rate varies by LGU.
</RegionalNote>

### Why is my Shopee or Lazada payout so much lower than my item price?

Because four or five fees are deducted before the money reaches your wallet, and they are charged on different bases. The transaction fee runs on the shipping-inclusive total, the commission runs on your item price, any free-shipping or cashback program adds a few percent each, and a 1 percent tax may be withheld on top. Stack a 2.24 percent transaction fee, a 4 percent commission, a 5.6 percent free-shipping fee, and a 3.36 percent cashback fee, and you have surrendered over 15 percent of the order before tax. The [fee calculator](/shopee-lazada-fee-calculator) adds the specific ones you have opted into so you see the net payout before you commit to a price.

### How much should I mark up to keep my margin after fees?

Decide your target take-home margin first, then gross it up for the fee stack, because adding a flat markup after the fact almost always undershoots. If your fees total 15 percent of the sale and you want a 20 percent margin on cost, you cannot just add 35 percent to cost; the fees are charged on the higher selling price, not on your cost, so the markup compounds. The reliable method is to compute the price that leaves your desired peso profit after the platform takes its percentage of that final price, which is exactly the back-solve the calculator runs so you are not pricing by trial and error.
