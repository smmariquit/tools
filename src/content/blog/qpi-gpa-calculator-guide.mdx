Students at Ateneo, La Salle, and a handful of other schools get a number called QPI or GPA instead of the 1.00 to 5.00 average, and the confusion is almost always the same: they treat it like a percentage grade when it is really a points-per-unit average. Quality Point Index and Grade Point Average are the same idea wearing different names, and once you see that a single letter grade is first converted into points before anything else happens, the whole system stops feeling like a black box. I built the [QPI/GPA calculator](/qpi-gpa-calculator) to do that conversion automatically, but it helps to know what it is doing on your behalf.

## Letter to points, then weighted by units

The QPI is computed in two moves. First, each letter grade becomes a quality-point value on a 4.0 scale, where an A is typically 4.0, a B+ is 3.5, a B is 3.0, and so on down to a failing mark at 0.0. Second, you weight those points exactly the way a weighted average works: multiply each subject's points by its units, total them, and divide by your total units. A semester of an A in a 3-unit course, a B+ in a 3-unit course, and a B in a 6-unit course gives you (12.0 + 10.5 + 18.0) over 12 units, which is a 3.375 QPI.

The exact point value attached to each letter is not universal, and that is the first thing to check. Some schools split the B band into B+ and B, others add an A- step, and the failing threshold differs. Plugging your grades into a generic 4.0 table when your school uses its own mapping is the quiet way people miscompute by a tenth of a point, which is precisely the margin that decides Dean's List standing.

## The part that catches people: a failing grade still counts

Here is the asymmetry that surprises students moving from a school that simply drops failed subjects. Under most QPI systems a failing grade enters the average as a 0.0 weighted by its units, and it stays in your cumulative QPI even after you retake the course. A 3-unit failure does not just cost you the credit; it parks a zero in your running total that a single good semester cannot fully erase. I have seen students assume a retake wipes the slate, then not understand why their cumulative number barely moved.

What actually happens to the old grade depends on your school's policy. Some institutions average both the original and the retake attempt into the cumulative QPI permanently, while others let the higher grade replace the lower one for the cumulative figure. Find out which rule yours uses before you bank on a comeback, because the two outcomes are very different for anyone near an honors or scholarship cutoff. ## Why a 4.0 QPI is not automatically a US 4.0 GPA

Because Ateneo and La Salle already report on a roughly 4.0 scale, students applying abroad often assume their QPI *is* a US GPA and can be dropped straight onto an application. That assumption is the expensive one. Two systems can both top out at 4.0 and still disagree on what a letter is worth: the point value assigned to a B, whether an A- step exists, and where the failing line sits all differ between a Philippine school's table and a given US institution's. A "4.0 looks like a 4.0" shortcut quietly mis-states your record.

There is no single official conversion between any Philippine scale and a foreign GPA — not for the 4.0 QPI, not for the 1.00–5.00 GWA, and not for percentage systems. The bodies that actually produce the GPA a program will trust are **credential evaluators** like [WES (World Education Services)](https://www.wes.org/), [ECE (Educational Credential Evaluators)](https://www.ece.org/), and IERF, each applying its *own* methodology. They require your **official transcript plus your school's official grading scale**, sent directly by the registrar rather than by you ([WES's required-documents list for the Philippines](https://applications.wes.org/required-documents/philippines/) spells this out). Even WES's own [iGPA calculator](https://applications.wes.org/igpa-calculator/) is explicitly an estimate that the formal evaluation can supersede.

So treat your QPI as your school's number, not as a portable GPA. Ask your registrar for an **official certification of the grading scale**, do not self-convert your QPI into a US GPA and present it as authoritative, and check each program's stated requirement before paying for anything — some accept a self-reported figure, others insist on a WES course-by-course report. If you reference a conversion at all, attribute it to the evaluator's methodology, not to a number you computed yourself.

### Is QPI the same as GPA?

Functionally, yes. They are two names for a units-weighted average of grade points on a roughly 4.0 scale. The labels are mostly institutional habit, with QPI more common in some Philippine universities and GPA more common in others and in international contexts. The mechanics, letter to points to weighted average, are identical, so a calculator built for one handles the other.

### Why is my QPI lower than I expected after a good semester?

Almost always because it is cumulative and one earlier weak term is still being averaged in, or because a heavy-unit course with a middling grade outweighed several light courses with high grades. Try computing your term QPI separately from your cumulative QPI; seeing them side by side usually explains the gap immediately.
