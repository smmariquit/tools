The single most valuable decision a self-employed Filipino makes each year is also the one most people make on a vibe. The [TRAIN Law (RA 10963)](https://lawphil.net/statutes/repacts/ra2017/ra_10963_2017.html) lets freelancers, professionals, and sole proprietors choose between an 8% flat income tax and the regular graduated brackets, and the wrong pick can cost tens of thousands of pesos a year. The choice is not about which sounds simpler. It is about how much you spend to earn what you earn.

## The 8% flat rate, and who it is built for

The 8% option is a flat 8% tax on your gross receipts above the annual ₱250,000 relief, and it replaces both the graduated income tax and the separate 3% percentage tax in one move. So if you bill ₱800,000 in a year, you are taxed 8% on ₱550,000, which is ₱44,000, and you are done. No itemizing, no chasing receipts for deductions.

It is only available while you are a non-VAT taxpayer, meaning your annual gross stays under the ₱3,000,000 VAT threshold, and you have to formally elect it, usually on your first quarterly return for the year. Miss that election and you are defaulted into the graduated system for the whole year.

The thing the 8% does not do is recognize your costs. It is computed on everything that came in, not on your profit. That makes it close to perfect for a writer, a virtual assistant, or a consultant whose main input is their own time, because their expenses are tiny. It is actively dangerous for anyone running real overhead.

## The graduated brackets, and the 40% OSD shortcut

If you choose the graduated route, you apply the standard 0% to 35% brackets, but you get to subtract expenses first, and you also still owe the 3% percentage tax on gross. There are two ways to claim those expenses.

The Optional Standard Deduction lets you automatically deduct 40% of your gross receipts as expenses, no receipts required. Taxable income becomes 60% of gross, you run that through the brackets, and you add the 3% percentage tax. This is the sensible default for someone with moderate but undocumented costs.

Itemized deductions are the other path, and they make sense only when your real, documented expenses exceed 40% of gross. A business burning money on inventory, rent, salaries, and equipment can deduct all of it with official receipts, taxing only the genuine profit. The price is bookkeeping discipline; the BIR will want the paper trail.

## The crossover: where the smart choice flips

Here is the math nobody runs before deciding. The 8% on gross and the graduated rate on profit cross over at a specific expense ratio, and the gap on either side of it is large.

Take a designer billing ₱1,200,000 a year with light expenses, say 20% of gross, leaving ₱960,000 of profit. Under 8%, her tax is 8% of (1,200,000 minus 250,000), which is ₱76,000, flat. Under the graduated brackets that ₱960,000 profit lands in the 25% slice: ₱102,500 plus 25% of the excess over ₱800,000 is about ₱142,500, and she still owes the 3% percentage tax of ₱36,000, totalling roughly ₱178,500. For her the 8% is not close to optimal; it more than halves the bill, because she has almost no costs to deduct.

Now flip the expense ratio. A hardware reseller billing ₱2,500,000 with expenses of 85% of gross has only ₱375,000 of real profit. The 8% would tax her on ₱2,250,000, which is ₱180,000, brutal on a thin-margin business. Graduated with itemized deductions taxes the ₱375,000 profit (about ₱18,750 in income tax) plus the 3% percentage tax of ₱75,000, totalling around ₱93,750, roughly half the 8% figure. For her, electing 8% would be a serious mistake. The lesson is that the right answer is entirely a function of your expense ratio, and it deserves an actual computation, not a guess.

### What happens if I cross ₱3,000,000 mid-year?

You become liable to register as a VAT taxpayer, and you lose the 8% option going forward. The portion of income before crossing may still be handled under your original election, but once you breach the threshold you default to the graduated system and start charging and remitting 12% VAT. It is a hard ceiling, so high earners should watch it closely.

### Can a mixed-income earner use the 8%?

A mixed-income earner (someone with both a salaried job and a side business) can elect 8% on the business income, but the ₱250,000 relief is not deducted again, because it is already consumed by the exemption on the compensation side. Only the business or professional receipts get the 8%, with no extra ₱250,000 cushion.

### Do I still file quarterly if I am on the 8%?

Yes. The 8% is not a free pass on filing. You still submit quarterly income tax returns and an annual return; you just skip the separate percentage tax returns, since the 8% absorbs them.
