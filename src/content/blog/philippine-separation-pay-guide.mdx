The most expensive misunderstanding I run into is people who think separation pay is what you get for leaving a job. It is not. If you resign, you are owed your final pay, but in almost every case you are owed no separation pay at all. Separation pay is a specific remedy the [Labor Code (PD 442)](https://lawphil.net/statutes/presdecs/pd1974/pd_442_1974.html) gives employees when the company ends the relationship for an authorized cause, the kind of termination that is nobody's fault. Get that distinction wrong and the entire computation is moot.

## When separation pay is actually owed

The Labor Code splits dismissals into just causes and authorized causes. Just causes are employee fault: serious misconduct, willful disobedience, gross neglect, fraud, and the like. A valid just-cause dismissal carries no separation pay. Authorized causes are business-driven and blameless: redundancy, retrenchment to prevent losses, installation of labor-saving devices, closure not due to serious losses, and termination because of a disease that cannot be cured within six months. Those are the situations that trigger the benefit.

The rate depends on which authorized cause applies. Redundancy and the installation of labor-saving devices pay the higher rate of one month's pay for every year of service, or one month's pay, whichever is larger. Retrenchment, closure not caused by serious losses, and disease pay the lower rate of one-half month's pay per year of service, or one month, whichever is larger. A fraction of service of at least six months is counted as one whole year, which means someone with three years and seven months is paid as though they served four full years.

| Authorized cause | Separation pay rate |
|---|---|
| Redundancy | 1 month pay per year of service (min. 1 month) |
| Installation of labor-saving devices | 1 month pay per year of service (min. 1 month) |
| Retrenchment to prevent losses | 1/2 month pay per year of service (min. 1 month) |
| Closure not due to serious losses | 1/2 month pay per year of service (min. 1 month) |
| Disease | 1/2 month pay per year of service (min. 1 month) |

## The "half month" that is not actually fifteen days

Here is the nuance that catches even HR practitioners. When the law says "one-half month pay," the Supreme Court has read that to mean more than just fifteen days of basic salary. The accepted construction folds in the proportionate 13th month pay (one-twelfth of a month) and the cash equivalent of the five-day service incentive leave, which works the half-month figure up to roughly 22.5 days of pay per year of service rather than a flat fifteen.

The peso difference is real. Take someone earning ₱30,000 a month, retrenched after ten years of service, where the daily rate works out to about ₱1,379 on the 21.75 divisor. Computed as a naive fifteen days per year, you get ₱1,379 × 15 × 10, which is about ₱206,900. Computed correctly at 22.5 days, it is ₱1,379 × 22.5 × 10, about ₱310,300. That is a six-figure gap created entirely by how the words "half month" are interpreted, so it is worth getting right before signing a quitclaim.

<RegionalNote>
Both rates lean on your daily rate, and for a minimum-wage worker that daily rate is a regional figure, not a national one. The RTWPB sets the floor per region — roughly **₱695/day in NCR**, **₱600 in CALABARZON and most of Central Luzon**, **₱540/₱500 in Central Visayas**, and rising to **₱540 in Davao** as of mid-2026 — so an identical tenure and cause can yield a larger separation package in Metro Manila than in the provinces, purely from the wage base. The authorized-cause rules and the 22.5-day construction are national; the daily rate underneath is regional. Confirm your region's current wage order at [nwpc.dole.gov.ph](https://nwpc.dole.gov.ph/).
</RegionalNote>

### Do I get separation pay if I resign?

Generally no. Resignation is a voluntary act by the employee, and the Labor Code reserves separation pay for company-initiated terminations under authorized causes. What you do receive when you resign is your final pay, which is a different package: unpaid salary, the prorated 13th month, the cash value of unused convertible leaves, and any tax refund. The only common exception is when a company policy, an employment contract, or a collective bargaining agreement specifically promises separation pay on resignation, in which case the contract controls.

### Is separation pay taxable?

Separation pay received because of causes beyond the employee's control, which is exactly what authorized-cause terminations are, is exempt from income tax. That covers redundancy, retrenchment, closure, and disease. By contrast, an amount labeled "separation pay" that is really a negotiated exit package on a voluntary resignation does not automatically enjoy the same exemption, because the exemption hinges on the separation being involuntary.
