When you leave a job in the Philippines, whether you resigned or were let go, what people loosely call "backpay" is really your final pay: a small stack of components added together, then reduced by whatever you still owe the company. The Department of Labor's guidance (Labor Advisory No. 06-20) sets the expectation that final pay is released within 30 days of separation unless a more favorable company policy or agreement applies. The 30 days is the easy part to remember. The components are where the real arithmetic lives.

## What actually goes into the total

Your final pay is generally the sum of your unpaid salary for days already worked, your prorated 13th month pay for the year, the cash conversion of unused convertible leaves, any tax refund, and separation pay if an authorized-cause termination applies. From that gross figure the employer is entitled to deduct legitimate liabilities, and only then do you see the net check.

The unpaid salary piece starts with your daily rate, and this is where the first quiet judgment call hides. To convert a monthly salary into a daily rate, payroll divides by a factor that represents the paid days in a month. A common divisor is 21.75 for employees working five days a week, and 26 for those on a six-day week, though some companies use other factors depending on how they treat rest days and holidays. Whatever the divisor, the unpaid salary is your daily rate times the days you actually worked in your final cutoff.

<RegionalNote>
One input here is quietly regional: the daily rate. For minimum-wage workers the floor is set per region by the RTWPB, not nationally — about **₱695/day in NCR**, **₱600 in CALABARZON and most of Central Luzon**, **₱540/₱500 in Central Visayas**, and rising to **₱540 in Davao** as of mid-2026. Since the unpaid-salary, leave-conversion, and separation-pay pieces all build on that daily rate, the same formula produces a bigger final check in Metro Manila than in a lower-wage region for an otherwise identical job. The 30-day release rule and the component list are national; the wage base is not — check your region's current order at [nwpc.dole.gov.ph](https://nwpc.dole.gov.ph/).
</RegionalNote>

The prorated 13th month follows the same logic as the annual benefit: total basic salary you earned during the calendar year, divided by twelve. Leave mid-year and you still take a real slice of it. Unused leaves convert to cash at your daily rate, and the law guarantees at least the five-day service incentive leave is convertible, with anything beyond that depending on company policy.

| Component | How it is computed |
|---|---|
| Unpaid salary | Daily rate × days worked in the final cutoff |
| Prorated 13th month | Total basic salary earned for the year ÷ 12 |
| Leave conversion | Daily rate × unused convertible leave days |
| Tax refund | Over-withheld tax returned at separation |
| Separation pay | Only for authorized-cause terminations |

## The deductions, and the tax refund people forget to expect

The same employer who owes you money can also subtract from it. Outstanding company loans and cash advances, your share of the final month's SSS, PhilHealth, and Pag-IBIG, any remaining income tax due, and penalties for AWOL or for not completing the required notice period can all come out of the gross. None of this is the company being petty; it is the settling of a ledger.

The component most people overlook works in your favor. Withholding tax is deducted each cutoff as though you would earn that salary for the entire year, against the annual ₱250,000 exemption under the [TRAIN Law (RA 10963)](https://lawphil.net/statutes/repacts/ra2017/ra_10963_2017.html). Resign in June and your actual annual income lands far below what your monthly withholding assumed, which usually means too much tax was taken and your final pay includes a refund. I have seen people brace for a thin last check and get pleasantly surprised by the tax refund line, precisely because the exemption is annual but the withholding was front-loaded.

## A worked example

Say you earn ₱30,000 a month, resign on June 15, and have five days of unused vacation leave. On the 21.75 divisor your daily rate is about ₱1,379.31. Ten days of unpaid salary in the final cutoff is roughly ₱13,793.10. Your prorated 13th month, having earned about 5.5 months of basic salary, is (₱30,000 × 5.5) ÷ 12, which is ₱13,750. Converting five leave days adds ₱1,379.31 × 5, about ₱6,896.55. That puts the gross final pay near ₱34,439.65, before any tax refund is added and before any loan balance is deducted. ### How long does my employer have to release my backpay?

The Department of Labor's Labor Advisory No. 06-20 set the standard at 30 days from the date of separation, unless a company policy, contract, or collective bargaining agreement provides a shorter or otherwise more favorable period. Delays beyond that are common in practice, often because clearance has not been completed, but the 30-day benchmark is the reasonable expectation you can point to.

### Is my backpay taxable?

It depends on the component. Your unpaid salary is ordinary taxable income. The prorated 13th month and other benefits are tax-exempt up to the ₱90,000 ceiling. Separation pay from an authorized-cause termination is exempt entirely. And the tax refund portion is not income at all; it is your own over-withheld money coming back to you.

If a divisor or a number here looks off for your situation, it may be that your company uses a different daily-rate factor or that a fresh advisory changed the timeline.
