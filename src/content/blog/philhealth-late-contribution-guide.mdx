The panic over a missed PhilHealth payment is usually worse than the bill. I get asked about this most by two very different people: an employee who noticed a gap in their contribution history, and a freelancer who let voluntary payments lapse for most of a year. The rules treat them very differently, and knowing which camp you are in answers most of the question before any interest is computed. Everything here flows from the [Universal Health Care Act (RA 11223)](https://lawphil.net/statutes/repacts/ra2019/ra_11223_2019.html), which made PhilHealth coverage compulsory and put the burden of timely remittance squarely on employers.

## If you are employed, the gap is usually not your problem

This is the part that surprises people. When you are formally employed, your employer deducts your 2.5% share and is legally required to remit it together with their 2.5% counterpart. If they remit late, or not at all, the law makes the employer liable for the missed amount and the interest on it, not you. Your coverage as a member is not supposed to lapse because your employer sat on the money. So if you spot a gap in your contribution record from a previous job, the honest first step is not to pay the penalty yourself; it is to raise it with PhilHealth and let them pursue the employer. I have watched people quietly shoulder a former employer's delinquency out of fear, which is exactly the outcome RA 11223 was written to prevent.

The premium itself in 2026 is 5% of monthly basic salary, with a floor of ₱10,000 and a ceiling of ₱100,000. That floor means even a probationary hire on a low salary is treated as if they earned at least ₱10,000, so the minimum premium is ₱500 a month, split into ₱250 each. The ceiling caps the premium at ₱5,000 a month no matter how high the salary climbs.

## If you are a voluntary payer, the interest is on you

Self-employed members, freelancers, and OFWs are direct contributors with no employer to absorb the penalty, so a lapse here is genuinely yours to settle. The estimator on this site uses the common convention of 1% interest per month on the missed premium for delays up to six months, stepping to 2% per month once you are seven months late or more. On a ₱500 monthly premium that you let slide for eight months, the interest works out to roughly ₱80 (2% times eight months times ₱500), bringing the settlement to about ₱580 for that month. The amounts are small per month, but they compound across several missed months and several members in a household.

The sharper issue for voluntary payers is not the peso penalty, it is eligibility. PhilHealth generally expects your contributions to be current before it will pay benefits on a confinement, and there are limits on how far back you can retroactively pay to plug a gap. Letting payments lapse and then trying to pay everything the week before a planned hospitalization is the classic mistake. Keep voluntary contributions current, or at minimum settle them well ahead of any expected claim.

### Will I lose my PhilHealth coverage if I miss a payment?

As an employed member, no; your membership stands and the employer carries the liability. As a voluntary member, your membership does not vanish either, but your ability to claim benefits depends on having paid the required contributions for the applicable period, so a long gap can leave a specific claim unfunded even though you are still "a member."

### Can I just pay all my missed months in one go?

Voluntary members can settle arrears, but there are rules on retroactive payment windows, and longer gaps attract the higher 2% monthly interest. The practical move is to settle as soon as you notice the gap rather than waiting, because both the interest tier and the retroactive limits work against you the longer you delay.
