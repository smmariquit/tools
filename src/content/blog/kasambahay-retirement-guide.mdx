People assume a domestic worker's retirement is purely an SSS matter, but there is a second, separate entitlement that households almost never budget for. On top of whatever SSS pension the kasambahay has earned through contributions, the employer can owe a statutory retirement pay directly, under the [Retirement Pay Law (RA 7641)](https://lawphil.net/statutes/repacts/ra1992/ra_7641_1992.html), which amended the [Labor Code (PD 442)](https://lawphil.net/statutes/presdecs/pd1974/pd_442_1974.html). The [Batas Kasambahay (RA 10361)](https://lawphil.net/statutes/repacts/ra2013/ra_10361_2013.html) confirms that domestic workers are covered employees, so the retirement rule reaches the household setting.

## Who qualifies, and when

Absent a more favorable retirement plan or agreement, RA 7641 sets optional retirement at age 60 and compulsory retirement at 65, in both cases requiring at least five years of service with the same employer. A kasambahay who has worked for a family for, say, twelve years and reaches 60 can opt to retire and claim the benefit; one who hits 65 is retired compulsorily and the benefit becomes due. Less than five years of service and the statutory retirement pay does not attach, though the SSS side is governed separately by contributions, not tenure.

The five-year threshold is what most households overlook when they have kept the same helper for a long stretch. The obligation is not triggered by the SSS retirement; it is a distinct duty of the employer, and it exists even if SSS contributions were paid faithfully all along.

## The "one-half month salary" that is really 22.5 days

The headline rate is at least one-half month salary for every year of service, with a fraction of at least six months counted as a whole year. The trap is identical to the one in separation pay: "one-half month salary" is not fifteen days. The Supreme Court has read it to include the fifteen days of pay, plus one-twelfth of the 13th month pay, plus the cash equivalent of the five-day service incentive leave, which together come to 22.5 days of pay per year of service.

A concrete case makes the gap visible. Take a kasambahay paid ₱6,000 a month for ten years, where the daily equivalent works out to roughly ₱197 (monthly pay annualized and spread across the days of the year; the exact divisor a household uses can shift this slightly). At the correct 22.5-day rate the retirement pay is about ₱197 × 22.5 × 10, near ₱44,300. Compute it as a flat fifteen days and you land around ₱29,500, understating the obligation by nearly a third. For a low-wage worker who gave a decade of service, that difference is not academic.

<RegionalNote>
Because this benefit is built on the kasambahay's monthly pay, the regional domestic-worker minimum quietly sets its floor too. The RB-DW wage orders fix a different monthly minimum per region — as of early 2026 about **₱7,800 in NCR**, **₱7,000 in Central Visayas**, **₱6,750 in CALABARZON**, and **₱6,500 in Central Luzon and Davao**. A helper kept at the regional minimum for the same decade therefore retires with a larger statutory payout in Metro Manila than in a lower-wage region. The 22.5-day rule is national; the pay it multiplies is regional — check your region's current RB-DW order at [nwpc.dole.gov.ph](https://nwpc.dole.gov.ph/).
</RegionalNote>

### Is retirement pay the same as the SSS pension?

No, and conflating the two is the most common mistake. The SSS pension is funded by the contributions remitted over the years and is paid by SSS based on the worker's credited contributions and salary credits. The RA 7641 retirement pay is a separate, employer-funded obligation that exists under the Labor Code independent of SSS. A retiring kasambahay can be entitled to both: the monthly SSS pension and the lump-sum statutory retirement pay from the household.

### What if there was no written retirement plan?

RA 7641 is precisely the default for that situation. It applies in the absence of a retirement plan or agreement, which describes nearly every household arrangement. If a more generous company-style plan or contract exists, that governs instead, but where nothing was put in writing, the statutory minimum of 22.5 days per year of service is the floor the household must meet.
