The hardest question in any interview is not about your weaknesses. It is "What is your expected salary?", and most people answer it with their last salary plus a vague bump, or a round number that sounds nice. The problem with that approach is that you are negotiating a gross figure while your life runs on the net, and the two are far enough apart that a salary which looks generous can still leave you short at the end of the month. I built the [budget calculator](/budget-calculator) to run that logic in reverse: start from what your life actually costs, then find the gross salary that covers it.

## You spend the net, but you negotiate the gross

When an employer offers you a number, that is your gross. Before any of it reaches you, SSS, PhilHealth, and Pag-IBIG contributions come out, and then withholding tax is computed on what remains. The wedge between the two grows as your salary climbs, because the contribution ceilings flatten out while the tax brackets keep stepping up. Here is roughly how the gap looks across common salary points (approximate 2026 figures, single, no extra deductions):

| Gross monthly | Approx. net take-home | The wedge |
|---|---|---|
| ₱25,000 | ~₱22,300 | ~₱2,700 |
| ₱35,000 | ~₱29,800 | ~₱5,200 |
| ₱50,000 | ~₱40,500 | ~₱9,500 |
| ₱80,000 | ~₱60,800 | ~₱19,200 |

The lesson is not the exact peso amount, which shifts with your circumstances. It is that a ₱50,000 offer is really a ₱40,500 life. If rent is ₱15,000, food ₱8,000, and utilities ₱5,000, you are budgeting everything else, transport, savings, family support, out of the ₱12,500 that is left.

## Working backward from your actual costs

The reverse approach inverts the usual flow. Instead of taking a gross salary and watching what survives, you list every recurring monthly cost, total them into the net pay you genuinely need, and then solve for the gross that produces it after all four deductions. The tool does this with a binary search against the same SSS, PhilHealth, Pag-IBIG, and TRAIN Law brackets behind the [salary calculator](/salary-calculator), so the gross it returns reconciles exactly with the net pay you would actually see.

The expense most people leave out is the one that hurts later: savings. If you want ₱10,000 a month going into an emergency fund or MP2, that is not a leftover, it is a line item. Treat it as a fixed cost in the list and the tool will quietly raise your target gross to fund it, which is the entire point. The same goes for the costs that feel invisible because they autopay, the streaming subscriptions, the gadget installment, the gym you mean to use, and the family support that is a non-negotiable for a lot of Filipino households.

### What expenses do people forget when computing their target salary?

The predictable ones, rent and food and transport, get remembered. The ones that sink a budget are the irregular and the automatic. Build in a monthly slice for an emergency fund even if you would not spend it every month, an HMO or insurance top-up if your employer's coverage is thin, the minimums on any loan or credit card, and the subscriptions and installments that leave your account without you noticing. Family support belongs in the list too, because pretending it is optional just means your "extra" money disappears anyway.

### Is the asking salary the tool gives me what I should say in the interview?

Treat it as your floor, not your opening line. The figure the calculator returns is the gross that lets your real budget break even with your savings goal intact, so quoting exactly that leaves you no cushion for inflation, a tax-bracket change, or a year where rent goes up. Most people add a sensible margin on top and let the negotiation settle somewhere above their true floor. The advantage is that you now know where the floor is, so you can hold the line when an offer dips below the number your life actually requires.
