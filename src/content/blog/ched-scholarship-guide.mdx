The single most common misunderstanding about government college aid is that students treat free tuition as if it pays for everything. It does not. The law that made tertiary education free, and the cash subsidy that actually helps with daily expenses, are two separate programs with different eligibility, and conflating them is how families end up budgeting for money that never arrives. I built the [CHED scholarship calculator](/ched-scholarship-calculator) to keep those two straight, because the gap between them is exactly where people get caught short.

## What free tuition actually covers, and where it stops

Free higher education comes from the [Universal Access to Quality Tertiary Education Act (RA 10931)](https://lawphil.net/statutes/repacts/ra2017/ra_10931_2017.html), signed in 2017. It waives tuition and other mandatory school fees for students enrolled in state universities and colleges, local universities and colleges, and state-run technical-vocational institutions. The word that matters is "fees." It covers what the school charges, not what life costs. Your dorm rent, your transport, your books beyond what the school provides, and your daily meals are not part of free tuition, and a student who assumes otherwise is in for a hard first semester.

The other boundary catches private-school students. Free tuition under the law applies to public institutions, so if you are enrolled at a private university you are generally outside the tuition-waiver part of the program entirely. That does not mean you get nothing, which is where the second program comes in, but it does mean the headline "free college" you heard about may simply not apply to where you are studying.

## The Tertiary Education Subsidy is the cash part

The law also created the Tertiary Education Subsidy, and this is the one that hands students actual money for living costs rather than just waiving fees. It prioritizes students from poor households, including those in the government's poverty database, and it can reach students in private institutions who fall outside the tuition waiver. The practical reading is that free tuition and the subsidy are not the same benefit, and the more financially constrained you are, the more the subsidy, not the tuition waiver, is the part you should be chasing.

The edge case worth planning around is academic retention. These benefits are conditioned on staying enrolled and meeting your institution's academic standing rules, so a failed load or a dropped semester can interrupt the support. Students who treat the aid as unconditional, then stumble academically, sometimes lose the very subsidy that was keeping them in school, which is the worst possible time for it to vanish.

<RegionalNote>
National aid is not the only layer — many cities run their own scholarship programs that stack on top of CHED free tuition and the subsidy. **Quezon City's QCSP** can reach up to **₱110,000 plus a ₱50,000 "QC Excel" stipend** a year for top tertiary students; **Makati's MCRSP** runs about **₱20,000–₱80,000 a year**; **Taguig** grants roughly **₱15,000–₱50,000 per semester**. Because national CHED/DOST aid is not itself an "LGU scholarship," it generally stacks with a city grant — though QC, for one, bars holding *another LGU's* scholarship at the same time. Eligibility is residency-based and the amounts change per cycle, so confirm the current terms with your own city's scholarship office.

### Is free tuition the same as a CHED scholarship?

Not exactly. Free tuition is a universal waiver of fees at public institutions under RA 10931, available broadly rather than awarded competitively. CHED also runs scholarship and grant programs, and the Tertiary Education Subsidy adds a need-based cash component on top. So "CHED scholarship" can mean several different things, and knowing whether you need a fee waiver or a cash subsidy is the first question to settle.

### Does free tuition cover books, dorm, and allowance?

No. The waiver covers tuition and mandatory school fees only. Living costs like housing, food, transport, and most learning materials are not part of it. Those are precisely what the Tertiary Education Subsidy is designed to help with for qualifying students, which is why the two programs should be applied for and budgeted separately.
