Most salary math runs forward: start with gross, subtract contributions and tax, arrive at net. But sometimes you need to run it backwards. A client wants you to receive exactly ₱50,000 in hand, or you are negotiating and you know the take-home you need to live on, so the question becomes what gross salary produces that net. This is called grossing-up, and it is genuinely harder than it looks, because you cannot simply add the tax back to the net.

## Why you cannot just add the tax back

The intuitive move is to take your target net, figure the tax rate, and add it on top. It does not work, and the reason is that the deductions are themselves a moving target that depends on the gross you are trying to find. It is circular. Your income tax under the [TRAIN Law (RA 10963)](https://lawphil.net/statutes/repacts/ra2017/ra_10963_2017.html) is computed on taxable income, which is gross minus contributions. So to know the tax you need the gross, but to find the gross you need the tax. Each affects the other.

The honest way to solve it is iteration. You guess a gross, compute the contributions and tax forward, see what net it produces, then adjust the guess up or down until the net matches your target. A calculator does this in milliseconds; by hand it is a few rounds of trial and error. There is no clean one-line formula because the graduated brackets are piecewise and the contributions have floors and ceilings that switch the arithmetic at certain income levels.

## The caps that make grossing-up jump in steps

Here is the edge case that makes the reverse calculation behave strangely. The mandatory contributions are not smooth percentages all the way up; they cap out. SSS contributions stop growing once your monthly salary credit hits the ₱35,000 ceiling. Pag-IBIG is effectively capped at ₱200 on the employee side for most earners. PhilHealth runs at 5% but only between a ₱10,000 floor and a ₱100,000 ceiling.

What this means in practice is that for a high earner, the contribution portion of the deduction is basically flat, so above a certain salary the relationship between gross and net becomes almost linear and easy to predict. Below that, where SSS and PhilHealth are still scaling with salary, the same ₱1,000 increase in net might require a different bump in gross than it would higher up. So if you are grossing-up for someone earning ₱30,000 versus ₱120,000, the conversion ratio is not the same, and assuming it is will leave you short.

A practical case: a freelancer who needs ₱50,000 net per month and wrongly assumes a flat 20% tax adds ₱10,000 and quotes ₱60,000. But the brackets are marginal and the contributions are capped, so the true gross needed is usually different, sometimes lower than the naive estimate for an employee, sometimes higher for a pure professional with no employer share. Guessing the rate is exactly how people underquote themselves.

### When do I actually need to gross-up?

Most often when a payment is meant to be net of tax, such as a foreign client who agrees to cover your taxes, a prize or honorarium promised as a clean figure, or an employer offering a guaranteed take-home. In each case the payer needs to know the larger gross amount that, after deductions, leaves the promised net.

### Is the gross-up the same for employees and freelancers?

No. An employee shares SSS, PhilHealth, and Pag-IBIG with their employer and is taxed via withholding tables. A self-employed professional pays the full contribution themselves and may be on the 8% flat option instead of the graduated brackets. Same target net, different gross, because the deduction structure is different.
