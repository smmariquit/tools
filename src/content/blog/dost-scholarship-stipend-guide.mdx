When students hear "DOST scholarship stipend" they picture one number landing in their account each month. The reality is a bundle: a living allowance, a tuition subsidy, a book allowance, and several smaller line items, each governed by its own rules. And underneath the money is a string most applicants do not fully register until later, a return-service obligation that ties you to the country after you graduate. I built the [DOST scholarship stipend calculator](/dost-scholarship-stipend-calculator) to total the pieces honestly, because the headline figure alone undersells both the benefit and the commitment.

## A bundle of allowances, not one stipend

The program traces to the [Science and Technology Scholarship Act of 1994 (RA 7687)](https://lawphil.net/statutes/repacts/ra1994/ra_7687_1994.html), which the DOST Science Education Institute administers. The support is layered: a monthly living allowance for daily expenses, a separate tuition and fees subsidy, a yearly book or learning-materials allowance, and additional items such as a clothing or uniform allowance, transportation support, group insurance, and a thesis allowance in the final year. Each is paid on its own schedule and capped at its own amount, so the true value of the scholarship is the sum, not the monthly stipend everyone quotes.

The peso amounts for each component are set by DOST-SEI and adjusted by circular over time, so the figure that was true two intakes ago may already be outdated. The practical advice is to total the components for your specific batch rather than rely on a number a senior mentioned, because a stale living allowance figure is the kind of thing that quietly throws off a year's budget. Treat the institute's current guidelines as authoritative for the exact numbers.

## The catch: priority courses and return service

Here is the part that reshapes the decision. The scholarship is not for any degree you like; it funds students enrolled in priority science, technology, engineering, and mathematics programs, because the point of the law is to grow the country's S&T workforce. If your intended course is not on the priority list, the scholarship simply does not apply, no matter how strong your grades are.

The bigger string is the return-service obligation. In exchange for the support, you commit to working in the Philippines for a period typically equal to the number of years you were a scholar, and leaving early can convert the grant into a debt you have to repay. I have seen students treat this as a formality at signing, then feel genuinely trapped years later when a job abroad appears. It is a fair deal, the public paid for your education and asks for your early career in return, but it is a real obligation to weigh before you accept, not after.

<RegionalNote>
The return-service-bound DOST package is national, but it is not the only aid you can hold — many LGUs run city scholarships that stack on top. **Quezon City's QCSP** can reach up to **₱110,000 plus a ₱50,000 "QC Excel" stipend** a year, **Makati's MCRSP** about **₱20,000–₱80,000 a year**, and **Taguig** roughly **₱15,000–₱50,000 per semester**. A national DOST grant is generally not treated as an "LGU scholarship," so it usually stacks with a city one (QC, for instance, only bars holding *another LGU's* scholarship), but confirm against both your DOST agreement and the city's rules. These programs are residency-based and the amounts shift per cycle — check your city's scholarship office for the current terms.
</RegionalNote>

### How much is the DOST monthly stipend?

It is a monthly living allowance set by DOST-SEI, separate from the tuition subsidy and the book allowance, and the exact amount is revised periodically by circular. Rather than trust a figure from an older batch, confirm the current monthly allowance and the other component caps for your intake year, since the total package is what actually determines what you receive.

### What happens if I do not finish or I leave the country?

The return-service obligation generally requires you to work in the Philippines for a period matching your years as a scholar, and failing to render that service, or not completing the degree, can trigger repayment of the support you received. The specifics depend on your scholarship agreement, so read that contract carefully before signing rather than after a conflict arises.
