Every Philippine summer, the same shock lands in inboxes everywhere: a Meralco bill that jumped from ₱2,000 to ₱5,000 without any new appliance in the house. The bill did not lie and the meter is rarely broken. What changed is hours, the aircon ran longer, and electricity is billed on how long things run, not just what you own. Once you can estimate each appliance's consumption, the spike stops being a mystery and becomes a list of decisions. I built the [electric bill calculator](/electric-bill-calculator) to do this arithmetic, and the logic below is all you need to predict your own bill.

## The whole formula is wattage times hours times rate

Electricity is billed in kilowatt-hours. A kilowatt-hour is a thousand watts of power drawn for one hour, so to estimate any appliance you need three numbers: its wattage, the hours per day it runs, and your rate per kWh. Multiply wattage by daily hours, divide by 1,000 to get daily kWh, then multiply by 30 days and by your rate. In Metro Manila that rate has been hovering somewhere around ₱11 to ₱13 per kWh depending on the month and the generation charge, so it moves, and you should pull the current figure off your latest bill rather than trusting a year-old number. A 1,000-watt appliance running 8 hours a day is 8 kWh daily, 240 kWh a month, which at ₱12 is roughly ₱2,880 from a single device.

<RegionalNote>
The per-kWh rate is not a national number — it depends entirely on your distribution utility, and the gap between them is large. Recent all-in residential rates run about **₱14.48/kWh on Meralco** (NCR, June 2026), versus roughly **₱11.65 on BENECO** (Baguio), **₱12.57 on VECO** (Metro Cebu, approx.), **₱10.35 on Davao Light** (approx.), and **₱8.84 on CEPALCO** (Cagayan de Oro, approx.). So the same 240 kWh costs noticeably less in Mindanao than in Manila. All of these change monthly with generation and transmission charges, and the non-Meralco figures are approximate — pull the current rate from your own provider's latest advisory.
</RegionalNote>

## The aircon almost always wins

In a typical Filipino home the air conditioner is the headline cost, and it is not close. A standard 1HP non-inverter unit draws on the order of 1,000 watts, and running it eight hours a night through summer can add ₱2,500 to ₱3,500 to a single month's bill on its own. This is why the inverter-versus-non-inverter question is the most consequential appliance decision in the house: an inverter compressor modulates its output instead of cycling fully on and off, and over a season that can cut the unit's consumption by something like 40 to 60 percent. The catch is the upfront premium, so the honest math is whether the monthly saving pays back the higher sticker price over the years you will keep the unit, which for heavy users it usually does.

The refrigerator is the quiet runner-up precisely because it never turns off. It draws less than an aircon at any instant, but it runs 24 hours a day, every day, so an old, inefficient fridge can rival an aircon over a month. Water heaters and electric showers are short-duration but enormous-wattage, a 3,500-watt shower heater used by a family adds up fast, and a high-end desktop PC left on all day can quietly consume as much as a small aircon.

## Where your peso per kWh actually goes

The number that surprises people who read their bill closely is that the rate is not one charge. The largest slice is usually the generation charge, the cost of the electricity itself, which Meralco passes through from power suppliers and does not mark up; when global fuel prices or the spot market move, your bill moves with them, which is a big part of why summer rates climb on top of summer usage. The rest is distribution, transmission, taxes, and government-mandated charges. The practical takeaway is that a chunk of your bill is outside both your control and Meralco's, so the lever you actually hold is consumption, and the highest-leverage consumption is almost always cooling.

### Which appliance is making my bill so high?

Estimate each one with wattage times hours and the culprit usually announces itself. In the overwhelming majority of Filipino homes it is the air conditioner, because it combines high wattage with long runtime during exactly the months bills spike. If your bill is high year-round rather than seasonally, suspect the always-on devices instead: an aging refrigerator, a water dispenser with a constant heating element, or electronics left on standby. Running the numbers per appliance, rather than guessing, is the only way to stop blaming the wrong device and to see whether replacing an old unit would actually pay for itself.

### Does turning appliances off at the switch really save money?

For most small electronics the standby or "vampire" draw is real but modest, a TV, a charger, or a set-top box sipping a few watts around the clock, which adds up to a small but non-zero line over a year. Unplugging them is worth doing and costs nothing, but it will not rescue a bill blown up by an aircon. Spend the effort where the watts are: clean your aircon filters every couple of weeks so the compressor does not labor, set the thermostat a degree or two higher, and switch incandescent bulbs to LED, which use a fraction of the energy for the same light.
