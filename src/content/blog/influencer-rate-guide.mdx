Brands love to ask one question first: how many followers do you have? It is the worst possible basis for a rate, and creators who price on follower count alone leave money on the table or scare off brands with numbers that do not match their actual influence. A 20,000-follower account with people who genuinely act on its recommendations is worth more to an advertiser than a 200,000-follower account full of dormant or bought audience. The number that captures this is engagement rate, and it should anchor your pricing.

## Why engagement rate beats follower count

Engagement rate is the share of your audience that actually interacts with a post, usually computed as total engagements (likes, comments, shares, saves) divided by followers, expressed as a percentage. A 100,000-follower account averaging 1,000 interactions sits at 1%, while a 15,000-follower account pulling 1,200 interactions sits at 8%. To a brand spending on conversions, the second account is the better buy despite being a fraction of the size, because engagement is a proxy for trust, and trust is what moves a product.

A workable starting framework many creators use is a base rate per follower scaled by engagement, often somewhere between ₱0.50 and ₱2.00 per follower per post depending on niche, then adjusted up for strong engagement and down for weak. So a 15,000-follower creator at ₱1.00 per follower starts near ₱15,000 for a post, but an 8% engagement rate (well above the typical 1% to 3%) justifies pushing well past that. The framework is a floor to argue up from, not a law.

## Niche and deliverables move the rate more than size

Two creators with identical reach can command very different rates because of what they cover and what the brand actually receives. A finance, tech, or parenting creator whose audience makes real purchasing decisions can charge a premium that a general lifestyle account cannot, because the advertiser is reaching buyers, not just eyeballs. Usage rights matter just as much. A single in-feed post is one price; the same post the brand wants to run as a paid ad for three months, repurpose on their own channels, and keep using indefinitely is a different deal entirely. Creators routinely give away whitelisting and perpetual usage for free because they did not know to price it, and that is often worth more than the post itself.

Bundling is where rates compound. A reel plus three stories plus a static post is not three separate quotes added together; package it and you can both raise the total and make it easier for the brand to say yes.

## The income is taxable, and most creators underprice for it

Here is the part the rate-card guides skip: sponsored content income is taxable, and the BIR has been explicit that creators and influencers are covered. Whether brands pay you in cash or in product, it counts. As a registered self-employed individual under the [TRAIN Law (RA 10963)](https://lawphil.net/statutes/repacts/ra2017/ra_10963_2017.html), you can elect the flat 8% on gross receipts above the ₱250,000 annual relief, or use the graduated brackets with deductions.

The edge case that catches creators is payment in kind. A brand sends you a ₱40,000 gadget in exchange for a review, and you treat it as a freebie. The BIR treats the fair market value of that product as taxable income, the same as if they had wired you ₱40,000. So a barter deal that felt like a gift can create a tax bill with no cash attached to pay it. The practical move is to value every barter at its real worth, count it as income, and either negotiate a cash component to cover the tax or set aside cash from your other deals. Pricing your cash rate as if it were tax-free is the single most common reason creator income disappears faster than expected.

<RegionalNote>
The income tax is only the national layer. Once you register, your **LGU** also collects a **Local Business Tax / mayor's permit** at a rate it sets locally, renewed each January, on top of the BIR. If your work is tied to a licensed profession requiring a board exam you would also owe the ≤₱300/year **Professional Tax Receipt** (due Jan 31, government employees exempt), though most pure content creators register as a business rather than a profession. Either way the city's annual permit is a separate cost from income tax — confirm the bracket with your own city hall, since each LGU sets its own.
</RegionalNote>

### How do I calculate my engagement rate?

Add up the interactions on your recent posts (likes, comments, shares, saves), divide by your follower count, and multiply by 100 for a percentage. Averaging your last ten or so posts gives a steadier figure than a single viral or flat post. Anything consistently above 3% is strong on most platforms.

### Should I have one flat rate or negotiate each deal?

Keep a rate card as your anchor, then adjust per deal for usage rights, exclusivity, turnaround, and bundle size. A flat take-it-or-leave-it number either underprices your biggest opportunities or prices you out of small ones. The card sets the floor; the negotiation captures the extras.

### Do nano and micro-influencers really charge less?

Per post, yes, but per peso of ad spend they often deliver better, because their smaller audiences tend to be more engaged and more trusting. That is exactly why brands increasingly run campaigns across many micro-creators instead of one big name, and why a high engagement rate lets a smaller account punch well above its follower count.
