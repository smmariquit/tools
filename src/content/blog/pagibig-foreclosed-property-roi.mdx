Pag-IBIG's acquired-asset lists are full of properties marked 20% to 30% below market, and that discount is exactly why people lose money on them. The headline price is the one cost that is fully disclosed. Every cost that actually determines your return is buried in the phrase "as-is, where-is," and the tax math at the exit is computed on a figure you do not get to choose. Run the whole chain before you bid, not after.

## "As-is, where-is" is a cost line, not a slogan

Pag-IBIG sells these properties exactly as they sit. That usually means stripped wiring, missing fixtures, and roofs that did not survive years of vacancy, and Pag-IBIG fixes none of it. Your real capital is the purchase price plus your honest repair estimate. A ₱1,000,000 unit that needs ₱300,000 to be livable is a ₱1,300,000 investment, and that is the number every return below should be measured against, not the ₱1,000,000 on the listing.

The cost that catches people off guard is not in the building, though. "Where-is" can mean occupied. Some acquired assets still have the former owner or informal occupants inside, and the buyer inherits the time, legal expense, and discomfort of recovering possession. I treat possible eviction cost and months of lost use as a real line in the budget for any occupied unit, because pretending it is zero is how a "cheap" property turns expensive. Unpaid association dues and real property tax arrears can also ride along with the title, so confirm what you are absorbing before you commit.

## Strategy one: buy and hold for rental yield

If you intend to rent it out, the number that matters is gross rental yield, the annual rent divided by your total investment, times 100. In most Philippine markets a healthy figure sits around 6% to 8%. If your computed yield comes in under 5%, the property is effectively overpriced for an income play, and the same capital parked in something like tax-free Pag-IBIG MP2 may quietly beat it without tenants, repairs, or vacancy risk. The discipline is to compute yield against your full ₱1,300,000, not the discounted sticker, because the repairs are real money that earns nothing on its own.

## Strategy two: fix and flip, where the tax surprises you

Flipping means you are now the seller, and the exit fees are larger than first-timers expect. The Bureau of Internal Revenue applies a 6% capital gains tax, and the catch is the base: it is charged on the gross selling price or the zonal value, whichever is higher. So even if you sell cheap to move the unit fast, the BIR can still tax you on the higher zonal value of the lot. On top of that comes documentary stamp tax of 1.5% and, unless you found the buyer yourself, a broker's commission around 5%. Those exit costs alone can erase 12% or more of your selling price before you even subtract what you put in.

Put numbers on it. Buy at ₱1,000,000, spend ₱300,000 on repairs, and sell at ₱1,800,000. The 6% capital gains tax on that ₱1,800,000 is ₱108,000, documentary stamp tax adds about ₱27,000, and a 5% broker's fee is ₱90,000. Your net profit is roughly ₱275,000, not the ₱500,000 the price difference suggested. The official rate basis lives on [bir.gov.ph](https://www.bir.gov.ph/), and the zonal values it taxes against are published per location.

<RegionalNote>
There is one more exit cost that varies by location: the **local transfer tax** under RA 7160 §135, separate from the national CGT and DST. It runs **up to 0.5%** of the price or value in a province and **up to 0.75%** in a city or Metro Manila municipality, and the title cannot change hands without it. On the ₱1,800,000 flip above that is roughly ₱13,500 in a city — small next to the CGT, but real, and easy to forget when you model the deal. On the buy side, also confirm the unpaid real property tax (amilyar) and association-dues arrears riding with the title, since those are set and collected locally too.
</RegionalNote>

### Who pays the capital gains tax, the buyer or the seller?

By long-standing custom the seller pays the capital gains tax on a sale of capital-asset real property, which means it is your cost when you flip. It can be negotiated to the buyer in the contract, but assume it is yours when you model the deal, because that is the default and the more conservative plan.

### Why is the tax based on zonal value and not what I sold it for?

The BIR uses whichever is higher between your declared selling price and the property's zonal value precisely to stop sellers from underdeclaring to dodge tax. If the lot's zonal value is high relative to your sale price, your tax can feel disproportionate to the cash you received, which is exactly why you check the zonal value before agreeing to a flip price.
