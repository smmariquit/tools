Every road trip group chat eventually arrives at the same question: magkano ang ambagan sa gas? The honest answer is that you can get within a few pesos of the real figure before you leave, as long as you have three numbers: the distance, your car's fuel efficiency, and the current price per liter. The guessing only happens because people skip the middle one.

## The formula, and the number people get wrong

Fuel cost is two short steps. First, divide your trip distance in kilometers by your fuel efficiency in kilometers per liter to get the liters you will burn. Then multiply those liters by the price per liter. A 250-kilometer run to Baguio in a sedan that does 12 km/L needs about 20.8 liters, and at ₱65 a liter that is roughly ₱1,350 of fuel one way.

The number almost everyone fumbles is the fuel efficiency, because they use the brochure figure. Manufacturer km/L ratings come from controlled tests, not from EDSA at 6pm. Real-world consumption in stop-and-go city traffic can fall 30 to 40 percent below the rated figure, while a steady highway cruise on NLEX or SLEX often beats it. If you plug the optimistic showroom number into your estimate, you will under-budget on exactly the trips that have the most city driving at either end.

<RegionalNote>
One input here is regional: the price per liter. Pump prices are not uniform across the country — fuel costs more the farther it travels from the import terminals, so **Mindanao and remote provinces typically pay more per liter than Metro Manila**, while prices everywhere move weekly with world oil and the peso. This is a commercial, freight-driven gap, not a tax or LGU rate, so treat it as informational: pull the current price at a station near your actual route rather than assuming a single national figure.
</RegionalNote>

## Fuel efficiency by vehicle type

These are realistic working ranges for common Philippine vehicles. Use the lower end if your route is traffic-heavy and the upper end for mostly-highway trips.

| Vehicle | Typical km/L |
|---|---|
| Motorcycle (150cc) | 25 to 40 |
| Small hatchback (Wigo, Brio) | 14 to 18 |
| Compact sedan (Vios, City) | 10 to 14 |
| Mid-size SUV (Fortuner, Montero) | 7 to 10 |
| Large van (Hiace, Urvan) | 6 to 8 |

The spread within a single row is the point. A Fortuner driven gently on the highway can touch 10 km/L, but the same vehicle crawling through Manila with the aircon fighting the heat can drop to 7, and that difference is around 40 percent more fuel for the identical distance. When you are not sure, budget toward the thirsty end and treat the leftover as a buffer rather than a surplus.

## Splitting the ambagan honestly

The simple split is total fuel cost divided by the number of people, and for a casual trip that is fine. Two things distort it, though, and they are worth naming before someone feels short-changed. The first is toll, which on a long Luzon trip can rival the fuel bill itself; a Manila-to-Baguio drive can rack up several hundred pesos in toll across NLEX and TPLEX, and that belongs in the shared pot alongside gas. The second is whether the owner started on a full tank and returns on a full tank, because if the car comes home near empty, the next fill is really part of this trip's cost and quietly lands on the owner alone.

The fair version is to total fuel plus toll, then divide. If the driver is also absorbing wear, parking, and the inconvenience of lending their car, some groups exclude the owner from the split entirely as a courtesy. There is no legislated rule here, only the social one, and stating the method out loud before the trip prevents the awkward reconciliation after it. The [fuel cost calculator](/fuel-cost-calculator) traces your route on a map, estimates the distance, and divides the total per head so the arithmetic is not a debate.

### Why is my actual gas spend higher than my estimate?

Usually because the estimate used the brochure fuel efficiency or assumed pure highway driving. Traffic, aircon load, a heavy load of passengers and luggage, and aggressive acceleration all push real consumption above the rated figure. Re-run the estimate with a lower km/L for the city portions of the trip.

### Should toll be part of the gas split?

Yes, if you want the split to be fair. Toll is a shared cost of getting everyone there, so totaling fuel and toll together before dividing is the cleaner approach. You can pull exact toll figures from the [toll fee calculator](/toll-calculator).

### Does a fuller car burn that much more fuel?

Noticeably on long climbs and from a standstill, yes. Extra weight costs the most in stop-and-go traffic and on ascents like the Kennon or Marcos Highway approach to Baguio, less so at a steady highway cruise. It is a real factor for a packed barkada trip but not worth obsessing over for a light city run.

If the sample figures here drift from current pump prices, that is just the market moving rather than an error in the method; fuel prices change weekly. If the math itself ever looks wrong, tell me through the [about page](/about).
