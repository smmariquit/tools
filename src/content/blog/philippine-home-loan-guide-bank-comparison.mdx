Almost nobody buys a house and lot in cash here, so the real decision is which mortgage to carry for the next two to three decades. The two paths are a commercial bank or the Pag-IBIG Fund, and they are not interchangeable. They differ on interest rate, on how long that rate stays fixed, and on who they are actually built to serve. Pick wrong and you either overpay on interest or get rejected for a loan you could have qualified for elsewhere.

The Pag-IBIG side of this runs on the [Home Development Mutual Fund Law (RA 9679)](https://lawphil.net/statutes/repacts/ra2009/ra_9679_2009.html), the same law behind the contributions already coming out of your payslip.

## Banks versus Pag-IBIG, where each one wins

The honest summary is that banks compete on rate for high-value properties while Pag-IBIG competes on accessibility and term length for ordinary earners.

| Feature | Commercial Banks | Pag-IBIG Fund (HDMF) |
|---|---|---|
| Max loan amount | Up to 80-90% of appraised value | Up to ₱6,000,000 |
| Max loan term | Up to 20 to 25 years | Up to 30 years |
| Interest rates | 6.0% to 8.5% | 5.75% to 7.25% (by fixed term) |
| Vetting | Strict, favors high income | Accessible, designed for members |
| Best for | High-value properties (₱5M+) | Low-to-medium cost housing |

The single most important structural difference is how long the rate holds, which is the repricing question. Pag-IBIG can lock a fixed rate for as long as 30 years, but a bank typically fixes its rate for only 1 to 5 years. Take a 20-year bank loan at 7.0 percent fixed for 5 years: your amortization is genuinely constant for those first five years, and then on year six the bank reprices to the prevailing market rate. If rates have risen, your monthly payment rises with them. That repricing risk is invisible at signing and very real at year six, which is why I tell people to budget a buffer above the initial amortization rather than treat it as fixed for the full term.

## A longer term is a smaller payment and a much bigger bill

The compounding over two or three decades is genuinely hard to feel until you see it. Take a ₱4,000,000 loan at 7.0 percent and watch what the term does. Over 15 years the monthly is about ₱35,953 and total interest lands near ₱2.47 million. Stretch it to 20 years and the monthly drops to about ₱31,012 but total interest climbs to roughly ₱3.44 million. Push it to 30 years and the monthly eases to about ₱26,612, while total interest balloons to around ₱5.58 million.

Read that last line again. On a ₱4 million loan, the 30-year term has you paying ₱5.58 million in interest alone, more than the house itself, in exchange for a monthly payment about ₱9,000 lighter than the 15-year. Neither choice is wrong; the longer term buys cash-flow breathing room that lets many families qualify at all. But the trade is explicit, and it is worth seeing in pesos before you sign for the longest term on offer just because the monthly looks comfortable.

## The number that decides your loan is your income, not the price tag

First-time buyers fixate on the property's price and the appraised value, but the binding constraint is usually your capacity to pay. Lenders cap your monthly amortization at a share of your gross income, commonly around 30 to 35 percent, after accounting for your other obligations. So a couple eyeing a ₱4 million unit with a ₱32,000 monthly amortization generally needs combined gross income in the ₱90,000 to ₱100,000 range to clear that ratio, regardless of how much down payment they have saved. I have watched buyers with a healthy down payment get a smaller approval than they expected purely because the amortization brushed against their income ceiling.

A larger down payment is the cleanest lever you control. Putting 20 percent down rather than the minimum shrinks the financed amount, which lowers both the monthly and the total interest, and it can be the difference between passing and failing the income ratio. For Pag-IBIG specifically, eligibility also assumes you have the required contributions in place, so the membership you have been quietly funding through payroll is what unlocks the loan. If rates drop sharply years into a bank loan, refinancing with another lender is a legitimate way to reset, though it carries its own fees and reappraisal.

### Should I choose a bank or Pag-IBIG?

If you are buying a high-value property and can pass strict bank vetting, a bank may offer a competitive rate, but with repricing risk after the fixed period. If you want a long, predictable fixed term and you are buying low-to-medium cost housing, Pag-IBIG's up-to-30-year fixed structure and accessibility usually fit better. Run both through the [home loan calculator](/home-loan-calculator) before deciding.

### Why was my approved loan smaller than the property price?

Almost always the income ratio. Lenders limit your amortization to roughly a third of your gross income after other debts, so your salary, not the price tag or your savings, sets the ceiling. A bigger down payment is the most direct way to bring the financed amount under that ceiling.

### What is the repricing period and why does it matter?

It is the length of time your bank rate stays fixed, usually 1 to 5 years, after which the bank resets it to current market rates. If rates have risen, your monthly payment goes up at repricing. Pag-IBIG avoids this with much longer fixed periods, which is its main appeal for buyers who want certainty.
