Working on a holiday in the Philippines is a great way to boost your paycheck, but the computation can be confusing. Are you supposed to get an extra 30%, or double your daily rate? It all depends on what *type* of holiday it is, and whether that day happens to be your scheduled rest day.

Let's break down the rules set by the Department of Labor and Employment (DOLE) so you can ensure you are getting paid correctly.

## 1. Regular Holidays (Double Pay)

Regular holidays (like Christmas Day, New Year's Day, Independence Day) have fixed dates and hold the highest premium.

*   **If you DO NOT work:** You still receive 100% of your daily wage (provided you were present or on paid leave the day before).
*   **If you DO work:** You receive 200% of your daily wage for the first 8 hours. (Your regular 100% + an extra 100%).
*   **If you work and it's your Rest Day:** You receive 260% of your daily wage. (An additional 30% on top of the 200%).

## 2. Special (Non-Working) Holidays (Plus 30%)

Special non-working days (like Ninoy Aquino Day, All Saints' Day) are more flexible and often declared by the President for long weekends. The "No Work, No Pay" principle generally applies here.

*   **If you DO NOT work:** You get 0% (unless your company policy states otherwise).
*   **If you DO work:** You receive 130% of your daily wage.
*   **If you work and it's your Rest Day:** You receive 150% of your daily wage.

## 3. Special (Working) Holidays

These are ordinary working days. You receive your standard 100% daily wage with no premium, even if it is a declared special working holiday.

## Overtime During Holidays

If you work beyond 8 hours on a holiday, the overtime rate is compounded on top of the holiday premium. 
*   **Regular Holiday OT:** Hourly rate × 200% × 130%
*   **Special Holiday OT:** Hourly rate × 130% × 130%
