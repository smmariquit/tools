The thing that confuses almost everyone about holiday pay is that there is no single holiday rate. The Philippines runs two completely different regimes, and which one applies depends on a Presidential proclamation issued months earlier that nobody bothers to read. A regular holiday can double your pay. A special non-working day can pay you nothing at all if you stay home. The arithmetic only makes sense once you know which bucket the date falls into, so that is where I start.

The rules all trace back to the [Labor Code (PD 442)](https://lawphil.net/statutes/presdecs/pd1974/pd_442_1974.html), specifically the articles on holiday pay and premium pay. The percentages below are the implementing-rule conventions the Department of Labor and Employment applies to those articles.

## Regular holidays carry the "no work, with pay" rule

Regular holidays are the fixed national dates: New Year's Day, Araw ng Kagitingan, the Holy Week days, Labor Day, Independence Day, the two Christmas-season days, Rizal Day, and the movable Eid observances. These are the expensive ones for employers, because the law says you get paid even when you do nothing.

If you do not work, you still receive 100 percent of your daily wage. If you work, you receive 200 percent for the first eight hours, your ordinary pay plus an equal premium. If that holiday also happens to be your scheduled rest day, the rate climbs to 260 percent, because a 30 percent rest-day premium stacks on top of the doubled rate. Overtime beyond eight hours is computed on the already-elevated base, so a regular-holiday overtime hour is your hourly rate times 200 percent times 130 percent.

The detail that quietly disqualifies people is the day-before rule. To collect the 100 percent for an unworked regular holiday, you generally have to have been present or on paid leave on the workday immediately preceding it. Go on unpaid absence the day before Christmas and an employer can lawfully withhold the holiday pay for December 25. I have seen this surprise daily-paid workers who assumed the benefit was automatic; for monthly-paid employees it usually is not an issue, because their fixed monthly rate is already presumed to include the regular holidays.

## Special non-working days run on "no work, no pay"

Special non-working days are the softer category, the ones a President tends to add for long weekends: Ninoy Aquino Day, All Saints' Day, the eves around Christmas and New Year, and the occasional one-off proclamation. Here the default flips. If you do not work, you generally get nothing for that day, unless a company policy or collective bargaining agreement is more generous.

If you do work a special non-working day, you receive 130 percent of your daily wage for the first eight hours. If it is also your rest day, that becomes 150 percent. Overtime on a special day is your hourly rate times 130 percent times 130 percent. The gap between the two regimes is genuinely large, and it is worth checking the proclamation before you agree to a shift: the same calendar slot might be a 200 percent windfall one year and a 130 percent day the next, depending entirely on how the holiday was classified.

<RegionalNote>
The national proclamation is not the only list that matters. Cities and provinces declare their own special non-working days: charter days, founding anniversaries, and local fiestas: by ordinance or local proclamation, and these carry the same 130 percent "worked" premium (150 percent if it is also your rest day) as a nationally declared special day. So your town fiesta can be a paid special day for you but an ordinary workday for someone one city over. Check both the annual Malacañang proclamation and your own LGU's ordinance for the year, because the local additions change annually.
</RegionalNote>

| Scenario | Regular Holiday | Special Non-Working Day |
|---|---|---|
| Did not work | 100% | 0% (no work, no pay) |
| Worked, first 8 hours | 200% | 130% |
| Worked on a rest day | 260% | 150% |
| Overtime rate (per hour) | hourly × 200% × 130% | hourly × 130% × 130% |

## The double-holiday case nobody plans for

Every few years two regular holidays land on the same date. Araw ng Kagitingan and Maundy Thursday have collided before, and the DOLE's guidance in those years treated the day as two regular holidays stacked together. The practical effect is steep: an employee who does not work can be entitled to 200 percent, and one who works the day can reach 300 percent for the first eight hours. It is rare enough that most payroll systems are not configured for it, which is exactly why it is worth flagging when it happens rather than assuming the software handled it.

### Is holiday pay already included in my monthly salary?

For monthly-paid employees, the regular holidays are generally considered already factored into the fixed monthly rate, which is why you do not see a separate line item for an unworked regular holiday. Daily-paid and "no work, no pay" employees are the ones who feel the rules directly, because their pay tracks days actually worked. If you are monthly-paid and you actually report for work on a regular holiday, you are still owed the additional premium on top of your salary for the hours worked.

### What happens if a holiday falls on my rest day but I do not work?

For a regular holiday that lands on your rest day and you stay home, you are still entitled to the 100 percent unworked-holiday pay, subject to the day-before presence rule. For a special non-working day on your rest day with no work rendered, the no-work-no-pay default still applies, so you generally receive nothing for it unless your company grants more.
