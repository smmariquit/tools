Landing a $1,000 contract feels like landing ₱58,000. It is not, and the gap is wider than most new freelancers expect. By the time the money clears into a Philippine account, three separate cuts have already happened, and only one of them is the tax everyone worries about. Knowing the size of each is the difference between pricing your work properly and quietly working at a discount you never agreed to.

<AdBanner dataAdSlot="blog-in-article-1" />

## The platform fee

Upwork used to run a sliding scale that dropped from 20% to 10% to 5% as you billed a client more over time, which rewarded long client relationships. It has since flattened that into a single 10% freelancer service fee on contracts. Bill $1,000 and $100 is gone immediately, leaving $900 in your Upwork balance before you have touched a bank. Other platforms structure this differently, but the principle holds: assume a tenth of your headline rate belongs to the marketplace, and price accordingly.

## The forex spread, which nobody warns you about

This is the quiet one. When you withdraw that $900 to a local bank, you do not get the exchange rate you see on Google. Banks apply a foreign exchange spread, a margin baked into the rate itself, so where the mid-market rate might be ₱58.00 to the dollar, your bank quietly hands you ₱57.20. It does not show up as a line-item fee, which is exactly why it goes unnoticed. On a $900 withdrawal an ₱0.80 spread is about ₱720 evaporated into the conversion. Over a year of monthly withdrawals that is real money, and it is the single best argument for comparing your bank against the wallets and remittance services that advertise tighter spreads.

## The BIR, and the 8% option

Once the pesos land, the Bureau of Internal Revenue has a claim, and as a registered professional the [TRAIN Law (RA 10963)](https://lawphil.net/statutes/repacts/ra2017/ra_10963_2017.html) hands you a choice. You can pay the regular graduated income tax, or you can elect a flat 8% on your gross receipts in place of both the income tax and the separate percentage tax. The 8% is only available while your annual gross stays under the ₱3,000,000 VAT threshold, and you have to formally choose it, usually on your first quarterly return.

The good news is that the same ₱250,000 annual relief applies, so the 8% bites only on receipts above that line. The catch is discipline rather than rate. Nobody is withholding this for you, which means every peso of it has to be set aside by you, every time a client pays, against quarterly and annual deadlines that arrive whether or not you saved for them. The freelancers who get burned are almost never the ones who did the math wrong; they are the ones who spent the tax money before the deadline came. Treat the 8% as not-yours from the moment it arrives and the BIR becomes a non-event.

<RegionalNote>
One more claimant sits below the BIR: your LGU. Registering as a professional means an annual **Local Business Tax / mayor's permit**, set per city and renewed every January, separate from the national income tax. Licensed professionals who passed a government board exam pay a **Professional Tax Receipt**, capped at ₱300 a year and due by January 31 to the city of practice (government-employed professionals are exempt). These are small next to the platform and forex cuts, but they are real recurring costs: check your own city hall's schedule, since the rate varies by LGU.
</RegionalNote>
