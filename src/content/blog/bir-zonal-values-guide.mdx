Every so often someone selling a property asks me why their tax came out bigger than 6% of the price they actually received. The answer is almost always the same: the BIR never promised to tax your selling price. It taxes the *higher* of your selling price and the property's fair market value, and for the fair market value it keeps its own scorecard, the zonal value.

## What a zonal value is

A zonal value is the fair market value the BIR assigns to land per street or vicinity, per classification (residential, commercial, industrial), in each Revenue District Office. The authority comes from [Section 6(E) of the Tax Code](https://lawphil.net/statutes/repacts/ra1997/ra_8424_1997.html), and the [TRAIN Law (RA 10963)](https://lawphil.net/statutes/repacts/ra2017/ra_10963_2017.html) tightened it: zonal schedules are supposed to be revised at least every three years, published, and made available to the public. The current schedules live on the [BIR zonal values page](https://www.bir.gov.ph/zonal-values) as per-RDO spreadsheets, which is exactly as pleasant to read on a phone as it sounds.

The practical rule, in one sentence: when you transfer real property, the tax base is the **highest** of (1) the price in your deed, (2) the BIR zonal value, and (3) the market value in the provincial or city assessor's schedule.

## The taxes that ride on it

Four national taxes use that "whichever is higher" base:

* **Capital Gains Tax, 6%.** Charged on the sale of a capital-asset property regardless of whether you actually made a gain. Selling at a loss does not exempt you; if the zonal value is higher than your price, the 6% applies to the zonal value.
* **Documentary Stamp Tax, 1.5%.** Technically ₱15 per ₱1,000 of the same base, rounded up per thousand.
* **Estate tax, 6%.** Real property in an estate is valued at the higher of zonal and assessor's value as of the date of death. The [estate tax calculator](/estate-tax-calculator) walks through the deductions that matter more than the rate.
* **Donor's tax, 6%.** Same valuation logic when you donate property; the [donor's tax calculator](/bir-donors-tax-calculator) covers the ₱250,000 annual exemption.

Underdeclaring the price in the deed is therefore mostly pointless. The BIR computes against its own number anyway, and it will not release the Certificate Authorizing Registration, the document the Registry of Deeds requires before the title transfers, until the tax on the correct base is paid.

## What zonal values are *not*

The most common mix-up: zonal values have nothing to do with your **amilyar**. Real property tax is a local tax computed from the LGU assessor's assessed value (market value times an assessment level), on a schedule your city or municipality sets itself. Two different governments, two different valuations of the same lot. The [amilyar calculator](/amilyar-calculator) uses the LGU side; the zonal value engine below uses the BIR side.

Also worth knowing: zonal values routinely lag the actual market in fast-moving areas, sometimes badly. A lot in a newly hot district can trade far above its zonal value, in which case your deed price is the higher figure and becomes the base. The zonal value is a floor, not a cap.
