There are two numbers people remember about SSS: the rate and the deduction. The number that actually governs your contribution is neither of those. It is the Monthly Salary Credit, and almost every confusing thing about your SSS line traces back to it.

The whole system runs on the [Social Security Act of 2018 (RA 11199)](https://lawphil.net/statutes/repacts/ra2019/ra_11199_2019.html), which set the gradual climb to a 15% total rate that 2026 finally reaches.

## What the Monthly Salary Credit really is

SSS does not contribute based on your exact salary. It slots you into a bracket rounded to the nearest ₱500 step, and that bracketed figure, the Monthly Salary Credit, is what every contribution and future benefit is computed against. Earn ₱18,300 and your MSC becomes ₱18,500. Earn ₱25,000 and it lands on ₱25,000. Earn ₱100,000 and it stops dead at ₱35,000, because that is where the table ends.

That ceiling is the single most misunderstood feature of the program. Once your salary is at or above ₱35,000, your SSS contribution is frozen. A raise will not move it, ever, and your take-home actually inches up because the deduction has stopped scaling while your pay keeps rising. I get a steady trickle of messages from people convinced payroll shorted them, when in fact the MSC cap is simply doing what the law tells it to.

On the 15% total, you carry 5% and your employer carries 10% plus a small Employees' Compensation fee. If you are voluntary, self-employed, or an OFW, there is no employer to split with, so the full 15% is yours.

## The WISP, which is savings disguised as a deduction

Here is the part worth knowing before you resent the bigger deduction. Any portion of your MSC above ₱20,000 does not go into the regular pension pool. It is diverted into the Workers' Investment and Savings Program, sometimes called the Mandatory Provident Fund, which is a separate account that earns tax-free dividends and that you can eventually claim. So for higher earners, a chunk of what looks like money leaving your payslip is really being parked in an investment with your name on it. It is one of the few mandatory deductions that is genuinely working for you rather than just funding a shared pool.

## Finding your exact bracket

The official per-bracket figures live on the [SSS contribution schedule](https://www.sss.gov.ph/), though it is published as a dense PDF that is awkward to read on a phone. The [SSS contribution calculator](/sss-contribution-calculator) takes your salary, snaps it to the right MSC, and splits out your share, your employer's share, and the WISP portion. If the bracket it returns ever disagrees with the SSS circular, that is my error to fix, not yours to absorb.
