De minimis benefits are the small, exempt perks the BIR lets your employer give without triggering tax: the rice subsidy, the uniform allowance, the medical cash allowance, and a short list of others. Most explanations stop at listing the ceilings. The useful part, the part that actually changes someone's tax bill, is what happens when a benefit goes *over* its ceiling, because that excess does not simply vanish. It lands in a different bucket, and that bucket has its own limit under the [TRAIN Law (RA 10963)](https://lawphil.net/statutes/repacts/ra2017/ra_10963_2017.html).

## The list, and the ceilings that define it

De minimis benefits are defined by Bureau of Internal Revenue regulations, and the list is exclusive: a perk is only de minimis if it appears on the BIR's enumeration and stays within the stated limit. The common ones, with their current ceilings, look like this.

| Benefit | Tax-exempt ceiling |
|---|---|
| Rice subsidy | ₱2,000 per month |
| Uniform and clothing allowance | ₱6,000 per year |
| Medical cash allowance to dependents | ₱1,500 per semester (₱250/month) |
| Actual medical assistance | ₱10,000 per year |
| Laundry allowance | ₱300 per month |
| Employee achievement award (in kind) | ₱10,000 per year |
| Christmas and major anniversary gifts | ₱5,000 per year |
| Daily meal allowance for overtime/night shift | 25% of the basic minimum wage |
| Monetized unused vacation leave (private sector) | 10 days per year |

The list is the boundary. A "transportation allowance" or a "communication allowance," however reasonable, is not on the BIR enumeration, so it does not get de minimis treatment at all and is handled as ordinary compensation unless another rule exempts it.

## What happens to the excess (the part people get wrong)

Here is the mechanism worth internalizing. A de minimis benefit within its ceiling is fully exempt and is not counted against anything. The moment a benefit exceeds its ceiling, only the excess is in question, and that excess is added to your "other benefits," the same pool that holds your 13th month pay. Under the TRAIN Law that pool is tax-exempt up to ₱90,000 a year. Cross the ₱90,000 line and the overflow finally becomes taxable income.

Picture an employee receiving a ₱2,500 monthly rice subsidy. The first ₱2,000 is exempt de minimis; the ₱500 excess each month, ₱6,000 over the year, is swept into the ₱90,000 other-benefits bucket. If that employee's 13th month and bonuses already sit near ₱90,000, the extra ₱6,000 from the rice subsidy excess can be exactly what tips them over the cap and into a taxable amount. So a benefit that felt entirely tax-free can quietly create a tax liability, because its overflow consumed the last of a separate ceiling.

### Are de minimis benefits counted in the ₱90,000 cap?

Only the excess is. De minimis amounts that stay within their individual ceilings sit entirely outside the ₱90,000 bucket and never touch it. It is the portion above each ceiling that flows into the other-benefits pool alongside the 13th month pay, and only when that combined pool passes ₱90,000 does anything become taxable. This is why two employees with identical gross pay can have different taxable income purely based on how their employer structured the perks.

### Can I just convert my salary into de minimis benefits to avoid tax?

Not freely. The benefits are exempt only within the BIR's enumerated list and ceilings, and only when they are genuine benefits rather than disguised salary. Repackaging basic pay as inflated "allowances" outside the list, or stuffing a category far above its limit, does not create exemption; it creates excess that is taxable once the ₱90,000 pool is full, and it can draw scrutiny. The exemption rewards modest, defined perks, not salary relabeling.
