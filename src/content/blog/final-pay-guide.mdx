Final pay is where a lot of employees discover, too late, that their last payslip was negotiable. It is not a single number your employer hands over out of goodwill. It is a bundle of separate entitlements, some of which you have to know exist in order to claim, and the gap between what gets released and what you were actually owed is usually made of the components nobody asked about.

## What is actually in the bundle

Your last salary is only the first piece. A complete final pay typically gathers up four things.

The most obvious is your unpaid earned salary, covering the days you worked in your final cutoff that have not yet been paid. Then there is your pro-rated 13th month pay, computed on the basic salary you earned from January up to your last day, under the same [Presidential Decree 851](https://lawphil.net/statutes/presdecs/pd1975/pd_851_1975.html) that governs the December benefit. Leave alongside it, if your company converts unused leave credits to cash, the monetized value of whatever vacation or sick leave you did not burn. And finally, for some departures only, separation pay.

That last one is where the misunderstandings cluster, so it is worth being precise.

<RegionalNote>
Several pieces in this bundle — unpaid salary, leave conversion, and any separation pay — are built on your daily rate, and for minimum-wage workers that rate is regional, not national. The RTWPB sets the floor per region: roughly **₱695/day in NCR**, **₱600 in CALABARZON and most of Central Luzon**, **₱540/₱500 in Central Visayas**, and rising to **₱540 in Davao** as of mid-2026. So the same exit, same tenure, can settle higher in Metro Manila than in a lower-wage region. The 30-day timeline and the components are national rules; the wage base underneath is set by your regional wage order — confirm the current one at [nwpc.dole.gov.ph](https://nwpc.dole.gov.ph/).
</RegionalNote>

## When separation pay applies, and when it does not

Separation pay is owed for what the [Labor Code (PD 442)](https://lawphil.net/statutes/presdecs/pd1974/pd_442_1974.html) calls authorized causes: redundancy, retrenchment to prevent losses, closure not due to your fault, or a disease that makes continued employment unsafe. In those situations the law sets the rate at either half a month or a full month of pay per year of service, depending on the specific cause.

What it is not owed for is just as important. Resign voluntarily and there is no separation pay, only the rest of the bundle. Get dismissed for just cause, meaning serious misconduct or the like, and again none is due. This single distinction is the source of most disappointed expectations I see; people conflate "I am leaving" with "I am entitled to separation pay," when the law ties it specifically to how and why the employment ended.

## The timeline, and the leverage hiding in it

DOLE Labor Advisory No. 06-20 set the expectation that final pay is released within 30 days of separation, absent a more favorable company policy. The detail worth internalizing is that most company policies anchor that clock to the completion of clearance rather than to your last working day. That makes the scope and speed of your clearance the thing actually worth negotiating before you sign anything, more so than the 30-day figure itself, which is rarely the bottleneck.

Two more practical notes. Your final pay components are taxed the same way they always were, so your pro-rated 13th month still rides under the ₱90,000 tax-free ceiling shared with your bonuses for the year. And separately from the money, you are entitled to a Certificate of Employment, which an employer must issue within three days of your request regardless of any clearance dispute; do not let it get held hostage to the rest of the process.
