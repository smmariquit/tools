The expensive mistake with ID photos is assuming the studio knows your agency's exact requirement. They often default to whatever roll they have loaded, and a photo that is off by a few millimeters, or correctly sized but with the wrong background, gets bounced at the counter after you have already paid and queued. The dimensions themselves are not secret, and once you have them you can crop your own at home for free. The sizes are worth knowing regardless of how you produce the photo.

## The three sizes you will actually be asked for

Three formats cover almost every Philippine requirement. The 1x1 is the small one used for school IDs, gym memberships, and minor company records. The 2x2 is the workhorse for major government applications like NBI clearance, PRC, and the Civil Service exam. The passport size is the odd one out, and it is where people slip, because it is not a 2x2. Here are the exact figures, including pixel dimensions at 300 DPI, which is the resolution most agencies expect for a print-quality photo.

| Format | Inches | Centimeters | Pixels at 300 DPI |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1x1 | 1 × 1 in | 2.54 × 2.54 cm | 300 × 300 px |
| 2x2 | 2 × 2 in | 5.08 × 5.08 cm | 600 × 600 px |
| Passport | 1.37 × 1.77 in | 3.5 × 4.5 cm | 413 × 531 px |

The detail to internalize is that the Philippine passport photo is 35mm by 45mm, taller than it is wide, not the square 2x2 many people hand in by mistake. Submitting a 2x2 where a 35x45 is required is one of the most common rejections, precisely because the two feel interchangeable until an agency measures them. When in doubt, the passport format is the one to double-check against the specific agency's instruction.

## Sized correctly and still rejected

Dimensions are necessary but not sufficient. Government agencies care just as much about what is in the frame, and a perfectly cropped photo fails on content all the time. The background must be plain white with no shadow falling on the wall behind you, which is harder to achieve at home than it sounds and is the most frequent reason a self-made photo gets refused. Attire should be decent, typically a collared shirt rather than anything sleeveless.

The accessory rules are where people get caught. Many government IDs want eyeglasses removed to avoid glare obscuring the eyes, and earrings and necklaces are often discouraged, while a neutral expression with both ears visible is the safe default. The deeper edge case is recency: agencies frequently require a photo taken within the last six months, so reusing a flattering shot from two years ago can fail an identity check even when the size and background are flawless. The photo has to look like you now, not the you that you prefer.

### Is passport size the same as 2x2?

No, and this is the mistake that causes the most reprints. A 2x2 is a 5.08 by 5.08 cm square, while a Philippine passport photo is 35mm by 45mm, noticeably taller than it is wide. They look close enough to confuse at a glance, but agencies that ask for one will reject the other, so match the exact format the specific application requests.

### Why do agencies reject photos with eyeglasses?

Because glare and reflections from the lenses, or frames covering the eyes, interfere with facial verification. Many government IDs therefore ask you to remove eyeglasses entirely so your eyes are clearly visible. Combined with the plain white background and a within-six-months recency rule, the eyewear requirement is one of the quiet content checks that fails otherwise correctly sized photos.
