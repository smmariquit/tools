The thing households underestimate when they hire a kasambahay is that they are not just paying a salary, they are becoming an employer in the legal sense. The [Domestic Workers Act (RA 10361)](https://lawphil.net/statutes/repacts/ra2013/ra_10361_2013.html), better known as the Batas Kasambahay, puts real obligations on the household: a written agreement, payslips, mandatory rest days, the 13th month pay, and registration with SSS, PhilHealth, and Pag-IBIG. The single rule that surprises people most is who actually pays those contributions, so I will get to that quickly.

## Who counts as a kasambahay, and what they are owed

The law covers domestic workers engaged in household work: the general house helper, the yaya, the cook, the gardener, the laundry person, and the family driver, among others. It does not cover family members of the employer, nor those who do household work only occasionally or sporadically and not as an occupation.

A covered kasambahay is entitled to a monthly minimum wage set by the Regional Tripartite Wages and Productivity Board for their region, so the floor in Metro Manila differs from the floor in a province. Beyond the wage, they earn the 13th month pay like any rank-and-file employee, a weekly rest day of at least 24 consecutive hours, and, after one year of service, five days of service incentive leave. The employer is also required to issue payslips and keep employment records, which is the part most informal household arrangements skip and later regret when a dispute arises.

<RegionalNote>
That monthly minimum is explicitly regionalized — the RB-DW wage orders set a different floor in each region, and the spread is wide. As of early 2026 the domestic-worker monthly minimum is **₱7,800 in NCR**, **₱7,000 in Central Visayas**, **₱6,750 in CALABARZON**, and **₱6,500 in Central Luzon and Davao**. The same live-in yaya is a ₱7,800 obligation in Manila but ₱6,500 in Davao — about ₱15,600 a year apart for an identical job, purely from the region. Confirm your region's current RB-DW wage order at [nwpc.dole.gov.ph](https://nwpc.dole.gov.ph/), since these are updated periodically.
</RegionalNote>

## The ₱5,000 rule for contributions

Here is the provision that catches almost every first-time employer. Under RA 10361, if the kasambahay's monthly salary is below ₱5,000, the employer shoulders the entire SSS, PhilHealth, and Pag-IBIG contribution, both the employer share and what would ordinarily be the employee share. The worker pays nothing toward their own coverage. Only once the salary reaches ₱5,000 or more does the normal cost-sharing kick in, where the kasambahay's share may be deducted from their pay.

The practical effect is easy to picture. A household paying a yaya ₱4,500 a month must fund the full statutory contributions out of pocket, on top of that salary. Bump the salary to ₱5,500 and the employer can begin deducting the worker's share under the regular schedules, the same way any payroll works. Households planning a raise across that ₱5,000 line should know it changes who pays the contribution, not just the take-home figure.

## How the contribution amounts are set

The contribution rates are the standard ones for 2026. SSS runs at a total of 15 percent of the Monthly Salary Credit under the [Social Security Act (RA 11199)](https://lawphil.net/statutes/repacts/ra2019/ra_11199_2019.html), PhilHealth at 5 percent of monthly basic pay, and Pag-IBIG at 2 percent each from employer and member, capped so the member share lands around ₱200. The wrinkle for low-wage household workers is the PhilHealth floor: premiums are computed as though the worker earned at least ₱10,000 a month, so a kasambahay paid ₱6,000 still has PhilHealth reckoned on the ₱10,000 base rather than on actual pay. It is a small peso amount, but it is the floor doing exactly what it was designed to do.

### Do I really have to register my kasambahay with SSS?

Yes, and it is not optional once an employment relationship exists. RA 10361 makes SSS, PhilHealth, and Pag-IBIG coverage mandatory for kasambahay, and the duty to register and remit falls on the employer. Skipping it is the most common compliance gap in household employment, and it leaves the worker without the very safety net the law intended, while exposing the household to liability if the worker later files a claim.

### Is the 13th month pay required for a kasambahay?

Yes. A kasambahay who has rendered at least one month of service is entitled to a 13th month pay, computed the same way as for other employees: total basic salary earned during the year divided by twelve. Leave or get hired partway through the year and the benefit is simply prorated to the months actually worked.
