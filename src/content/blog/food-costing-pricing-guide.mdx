The carinderia down the street sells adobo rice for ₱65 and looks busy every lunch. Whether it makes money is a completely separate question, and the owner often does not know the answer, because pricing by feel hides the slow leak. Food costing is the discipline of knowing what a plate actually costs to produce so you can price it on purpose instead of guessing. I built the [food cost calculator](/food-cost-calculator) to make that arithmetic fast, but the reasoning matters more than the tool.

## Food cost percentage is the number to anchor on

The core ratio is your food cost percentage: the cost of the ingredients in a dish divided by its selling price. If a serving costs you ₱24 in ingredients and you sell it for ₱80, your food cost is 30 percent. Most sustainable food businesses target somewhere between 28 and 35 percent, which leaves the remaining 65 to 72 percent to cover rent, labor, utilities, packaging, and the profit that is the entire reason you opened. To price from a target, you flip the math: divide ingredient cost by your target percentage. That ₱24 plate at a 30 percent target wants a ₱80 price; the same plate priced at ₱60 is running a 40 percent food cost and is quietly eating your rent money.

## The cost you forget is yield loss

Here is the trap that catches nearly every new food business. You buy a kilo of pork belly at ₱380 and divide by your portion size as if the whole kilo becomes food. It does not. Trimming, bone, fat you render off, and cooking shrinkage mean a kilo of raw meat might yield 700 grams of sellable, plated protein. Your real cost per usable gram is therefore ₱380 divided by 0.7 kilo, about ₱543 per usable kilo, not ₱380. Cost your portions off the raw price and every plate is underpriced by roughly a fifth before you have paid for a single sachet of seasoning. Always cost off the yield, the edible weight that survives prep and cooking, not the purchase weight.

## Theoretical cost versus what your kitchen actually does

There are two food cost numbers and the gap between them is where your profit leaks. Theoretical cost is what a dish should cost if every portion is exact and nothing is wasted. Actual cost is what your inventory says you really spent over a month, divided by sales. A theoretical cost of 30 percent paired with an actual cost of 38 percent is telling you that eight points of margin are walking out the door through over-portioning, spoilage, staff meals, or pilferage. On ₱200,000 of monthly sales, those eight points are ₱16,000 a month vanishing without a single line item to blame. The fix is not pricing higher; it is portion control, a written recipe, and counting your inventory.

<RegionalNote>
Ingredients and packaging are only the costs you control. Operating a food business also means annual **LGU** charges that vary by city: the **Local Business Tax** (mayor's permit), typically graduated on your gross receipts, plus sanitary inspection, garbage, and fire safety fees bundled into the permit. In Quezon City, for instance, restaurants pay roughly ¾ of 1% on gross receipts above ₱2,000,000 on top of the fixed permit charges. These recurring city costs sit in your fixed-cost base, so fold them in before judging whether a 30% food cost actually leaves a profit: and confirm the schedule with your own city hall, since each LGU sets its own.
</RegionalNote>

### What food cost percentage should I aim for in the Philippines?

The 28 to 35 percent range is the working target for most full-service and casual concepts, but it is not universal. A beverage-heavy stall can run a much lower food cost because drinks are cheap to make, while a premium steak or seafood concept might tolerate 40 percent because the absolute peso margin per plate is still large. The percentage is a guide, not a commandment. What actually keeps you solvent is making sure the peso gross profit per dish, the selling price minus ingredient cost, comfortably covers your share of fixed costs at your realistic daily volume.

### Should I include packaging and delivery fees in my food cost?

Keep ingredient cost and packaging as separate lines, because lumping them hides problems. Packaging for takeout and delivery, the box, the bag, the utensils, the rubber band, can quietly add ₱8 to ₱15 per order, and on a ₱120 meal that is real margin. Delivery platform commissions are a different and larger bite again, often 20 percent or more of the order value. If most of your sales come through delivery apps, price a separate, higher menu for those channels, or the platform's cut turns a healthy dine-in margin into a loss you only notice at month end.
