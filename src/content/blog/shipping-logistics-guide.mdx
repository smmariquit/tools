The first time an online seller loses money on an order, it is rarely the product cost. It is shipping, computed wrong. A parcel that "should" cost ₱90 to send arrives at the courier counter priced at ₱160, and the margin on a small-ticket item evaporates. The fix is understanding that a courier rate is not one number, it is a stack: a base fee for the route, an excess-weight charge, and a handling fee if the order is cash on delivery. ## How the rate is actually built

Start with the base fee, which is set by the origin-to-destination route. Intra-city is cheapest, Luzon-to-Luzon is moderate, and anything crossing to Visayas or Mindanao steps up because it involves sea or air freight rather than a van. That base fee typically covers the first kilogram, and every kilogram beyond it adds an excess charge, often around ₱50 per additional kilogram for domestic couriers. So a 3-kilogram package is the base route fee plus roughly two kilograms of excess on top.

The edge case that catches new sellers is the kilogram boundary. Couriers round up, so a parcel weighing 1.2 kilograms is billed as 2 kilograms, and a 2.1-kilogram parcel is billed as 3. Shaving even 100 grams off packaging, lighter boxes, less filler, can drop you a full billing tier. I have seen sellers add a heavy promotional insert to every order and wonder why their shipping creeped up across the board; the insert pushed a chunk of their catalog over a rounding line.

## Volumetric weight is the bill nobody predicts

Here is the single most expensive thing most MSME sellers do not know: couriers charge on the greater of your parcel's actual weight and its volumetric weight. Volumetric weight prices the space your box occupies in the truck, not what it weighs on a scale. The common domestic formula is length times width times height in centimeters, divided by a divisor (often around 3,500), giving a "weight" in kilograms.

Run a real case. You sell throw pillows, light but bulky, shipped in a 40 by 30 by 20 centimeter box. Actual weight might be 1 kilogram, but the volumetric calculation is 40 times 30 times 20, which is 24,000, divided by 3,500, which is about 6.9 kilograms. The courier charges you for 7 kilograms, not 1, and your "1-kilo" shipping estimate was off by roughly ₱300 in excess fees. Anything light and large, pillows, lampshades, snack packs, inflatables, gets billed on volume, and pricing it on the scale weight is how sellers quietly subsidize their own deliveries.

## Cash on delivery is a percentage, not a flat fee

COD is the default for a large share of Philippine e-commerce, and the handling fee for it is usually a percentage of the declared order value rather than a fixed amount, commonly around 2 percent with a floor of about ₱50. The implication that surprises people is that COD scales with the order: a ₱5,000 order carries a ₱100 COD fee, while a ₱300 order hits the ₱50 minimum and so pays a far higher effective rate. For low-value, high-frequency items, that minimum is a real drag on margin, and it is an argument for nudging customers toward prepaid payment on small orders.

There is a second, quieter cost to COD: remittance lag and failed deliveries. The courier holds your money until it remits the collected cash, often a week or two out, and a refused or unclaimed COD parcel still costs you the return shipping. Both belong in your pricing even though neither shows up on the rate card. ### Why is my actual shipping fee higher than what I quoted the customer?

Almost always one of two things: the parcel was billed on volumetric weight rather than its scale weight, or the weight rounded up into the next kilogram tier. Re-measure the box's dimensions and run the volumetric formula before you set a flat shipping price for a product.

### How do I lower my shipping costs as a small seller?

Tighten your packaging to reduce both actual and volumetric weight, since smaller boxes win on the dimensional formula. Steer small-ticket orders toward prepaid to dodge the COD minimum, and consider building a flat shipping fee into your product price only after you know the volumetric weight, not before.

### Is COD worth the extra fee?

For most Philippine sellers, yes, because a large share of buyers still prefer paying on delivery and removing the option costs you orders. The fee is the price of access to those customers, but it is worth steering low-value orders to prepaid where the ₱50 minimum bites hardest.

These rates are representative working figures rather than any single courier's official tariff, and couriers revise them and their volumetric divisors periodically.
