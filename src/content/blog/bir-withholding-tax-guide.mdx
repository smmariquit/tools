The line on your payslip labelled withholding tax confuses people because it does not match the annual income tax brackets they read about online. That is by design. Withholding is a pay-as-you-earn mechanism: your employer estimates your annual tax and collects it in slices every payday, so you are not hit with one enormous bill in April. The slices come from the BIR withholding tax tables, which are just the annual graduated rates from the [TRAIN Law (RA 10963)](https://lawphil.net/statutes/repacts/ra2017/ra_10963_2017.html) sliced into daily, weekly, semi-monthly, and monthly columns.

## What actually gets taxed, and what does not

The number your employer runs through the table is not your gross pay. It is your taxable compensation, which is gross minus the parts the law shields. Your mandatory SSS, PhilHealth, and Pag-IBIG contributions come off first. So does your 13th month pay and other benefits, up to the ₱90,000 annual ceiling. De minimis benefits within their limits are excluded too. Only what is left lands in the table.

This is why two people on the same ₱30,000 gross can show different withholding. If one of them has a higher Pag-IBIG voluntary contribution or a salary structure with more non-taxable allowances, their taxable base is smaller, and a smaller base can sit in a lower slice of the table. The headline salary tells you almost nothing; the taxable figure tells you everything.

The table itself mirrors the brackets you already know: the first ₱250,000 a year is exempt, which on a monthly table works out to roughly ₱20,833 of taxable compensation passing through tax-free before the 15% slice begins.

## The semi-monthly trap that inflates one payday

Here is the edge case that catches people who get a mid-month bonus or commission. Withholding is computed per payroll period, not smoothed across the month. If your commission all lands in the second cut-off, that single period shows a much larger taxable amount, gets pushed into a higher slice of the table, and the tax on that one payday looks brutal. Someone earning a steady ₱18,000 semi-monthly might see normal withholding, then get a ₱40,000 commission in one cut-off and watch the tax on that period jump far more than proportionally.

It feels like a penalty for earning more, but it is not. The period table assumes that ₱58,000 is your every-period income, annualizes it, and withholds accordingly. Come the December annualization (covered in the [tax refund guide](/tax-refund-guide)), the over-withholding from that spike is reconciled against your true annual tax, and the excess usually comes back as a refund. The period-level sting and the year-level truth are two different numbers.

### Why is my withholding tax higher than the annual bracket suggests?

Usually because a variable-pay period got annualized as if it were permanent, or because non-taxable items were not deducted before the table was applied. Withholding is always an estimate that the year-end computation corrects. If the estimate ran high, you get a refund; if it ran low, you owe the balance.

### Do minimum wage earners pay withholding tax?

No. Statutory minimum wage earners are exempt from income tax on their basic wage, plus holiday pay, overtime, night differential, and hazard pay. Crucially, this exemption can be lost. If such a worker earns other taxable income that disqualifies them, the exemption falls away, so payroll has to watch that line carefully.

### What is BIR Form 2316 and why does it matter?

It is the certificate of compensation and tax withheld that your employer issues each year. It is your proof of taxes already paid, and it is what a new employer needs if you switch jobs mid-year so they can annualize correctly instead of giving you the ₱250,000 exemption twice. Keep every one you receive.

The certificate side of withholding intimidates people more than it should.
