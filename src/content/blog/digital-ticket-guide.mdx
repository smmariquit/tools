Run a small event in the Philippines, a fun run, a church concert, a barangay fair, and you hit the same wall everyone hits: how do you check who actually paid without printing physical tickets or paying for a bloated ticketing platform that takes a cut of every sale. The answer most organizers reach for is a QR code, and there is a lot of confusion about what that code is and whether it is safe. I built the [digital ticket generator](/digital-ticket-generator) to make this trivial for a small event, but it is worth understanding what is happening underneath, because the magic is simpler and the pitfalls more real than people assume.

## A QR code is just text in a picture

There is no intelligence in a QR code itself. It is a visual encoding of a short string of text, nothing more. Scan one and your phone reads back whatever was baked in, which might be a URL, a few words, or for a ticket, an identifier like an order number plus the attendee's name. That is the whole trick: instead of storing thousands of tickets in a database that someone has to host and pay for, you encode each ticket's details into the code and let the scan read them back at the gate. For a one-off event with a few hundred guests, this removes the entire need for a server, an account system, and a monthly subscription.

## The forgery problem, and the signature that fixes it

The obvious objection is that if a QR code is just readable text, anyone can generate their own. They can. A bare QR ticket that simply says "Ticket 0042, Juan dela Cruz" is trivial to fake, and worse, trivial to duplicate by screenshotting and forwarding to ten friends. The serious fix is a cryptographic signature. The generator takes the ticket data, runs it through a secret key only you hold, and appends the resulting signature to the code. At the gate, your scanner re-runs the same check; if the data was altered or invented without your key, the signature does not match and the ticket is rejected. This is the same idea that secures website connections, shrunk down to a gate scanner, and it means a forged ticket fails validation even offline, with no internet at the venue.

## The duplicate-scan gap a signature does not close

Here is the edge case organizers learn the hard way. A signature proves a ticket is authentic; it does not prove it has only been used once. A genuine, validly signed ticket can still be screenshotted and the image shared, and every copy carries a perfectly valid signature, because they are all the same ticket. The signature stops counterfeits, not duplicates. Closing the duplicate gap requires the one piece of state a fully offline system cannot have: a record of which ticket IDs have already walked through the door. In practice that means either a single shared check-in list the gate staff mark off, or a lightweight scanner that remembers scanned IDs for the day. For a 200-person event, one person with the list on a phone is usually enough; for a larger gate with multiple lanes, you need those lanes sharing one record, or the same screenshot sails through two different doors.

### Do I need a paid ticketing platform for a small event?

For a few hundred guests, almost certainly not. The expensive platforms earn their fee on scale, fraud protection, and integrated payments, which matter for a 10,000-seat concert and are overkill for a parish fundraiser. A signed QR ticket you generate yourself, paired with a simple check-in list to catch duplicates, covers the actual requirement of most small Philippine events at zero platform cost. The honest reason to pay for a platform is when you need integrated online payment collection and reserved seating, not the QR codes themselves, which are free.

### Is it safe to put a guest's name in the QR code?

Treat the code as readable by anyone who scans it, because it is. Encode only what you need to verify entry, an identifier and perhaps a first name, and avoid baking in sensitive personal details like a full address or contact number that you would not want a stray scan to expose. Under the Data Privacy Act you are responsible for personal information you collect for an event, so the lighter the data in the code, the smaller your obligation and your risk. The signature protects authenticity, not confidentiality; anyone can still read the plain contents.
