The reassuring thing about the SSS pension is that you do not have to pick the right formula. The system computes your monthly pension three different ways and simply pays you the largest result, under the rules in the [Social Security Act of 2018 (RA 11199)](https://lawphil.net/statutes/repacts/ra2019/ra_11199_2019.html). Your job is not to optimize the formula. It is to understand the two inputs that feed all three, because those are the only levers you actually control.

## The two numbers that decide everything

The first is your Average Monthly Salary Credit, the AMSC. It is the average of your salary credits over the last 60 months, the five years, before the semester you retire. The word "last" is doing heavy lifting here: the system does not average your whole working life, only the run-up to retirement. That asymmetry is the single most useful fact in this entire guide, and I will come back to it.

The second is your Credited Years of Service, the CYS, which is the total number of years you contributed. Ten years, or 120 monthly contributions, is the threshold to qualify for a lifetime monthly pension at all. Fall short of it and SSS pays you a one-time lump sum instead of a monthly pension, which is a meaningfully worse outcome for anyone who lives a long retirement.

## The three formulas

Each formula favors a different kind of contributor, which is the whole point of running all three.

The first rewards longevity. It pays ₱300, plus 20% of your AMSC, plus another 2% of your AMSC for every year of service beyond the tenth. On an AMSC of ₱20,000 with 25 years in, that is ₱300 plus ₱4,000 plus ₱6,000, which comes to ₱10,300 a month. The longer you contributed, the more this one pulls ahead.

The second is just 40% of your AMSC, a flat ₱8,000 on that same ₱20,000 average. It tends to win for people with a high AMSC but relatively few years, someone who earned and contributed at the ceiling but only crossed the ten-year line.

The third is a floor rather than a formula: a guaranteed minimum of ₱1,200 for members with 10 to 19 years of service, and ₱2,400 for those with 20 or more. It exists to keep low-AMSC pensioners from falling through. On top of whichever figure wins, there is also a standing ₱1,000 monthly add-on, and pensioners receive a 13th-month pension every December.

## What this means for your last five years

Here is where the AMSC asymmetry pays off. Because the average is taken over the 60 months before retirement, contributions made at a high salary credit late in your career carry far more weight than the same contributions made early. Someone who coasts at a low MSC for decades and then maxes out their final five years can lift their pension substantially, while someone who maxes out early and lets their contributions lapse near the end gives back ground.

The practical reading of that is twofold. Push your salary credit as high as you can in the final stretch, and if you lose your job or move abroad before reaching ten years, switch to voluntary contributions and keep paying, because every additional year past the tenth also adds that 2% multiplier to the first formula.
