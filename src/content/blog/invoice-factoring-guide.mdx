You delivered the work, you issued the invoice, and the client's payment terms say 60 days. Meanwhile payroll is in two weeks and your supplier wants their deposit now. Invoice factoring solves that timing gap by selling your unpaid invoice to a financier today for slightly less than its face value, so you get cash now and they collect the full amount later. It is a genuinely useful tool for an MSME with lumpy cashflow, and it is also one of the easiest places to misjudge the true cost, because the fee is quoted as a small percentage that hides a large annualized rate. ## How the mechanics actually work

Factoring has three moving parts. The advance rate is the slice of the invoice you receive upfront, often 70 to 90 percent, with the rest held back as a reserve. The discount or factoring fee is what the financier keeps for the service, quoted as a percentage of the invoice and usually tied to how long until the client pays. When the client finally settles, you get the held-back reserve minus the fee. So on a ₱100,000 invoice at an 80 percent advance and a 3 percent fee, you receive ₱80,000 now, and when the client pays you get the remaining ₱20,000 less the ₱3,000 fee, netting ₱97,000 in total against the ₱100,000 you were owed.

## Why a 3 percent fee is not a 3 percent loan

Here is the part that separates a good decision from an expensive habit. That ₱3,000 fee feels like 3 percent, but you only borrowed the money for the 60 days until the client paid. To compare it honestly against a bank loan or a credit line, you annualize it. Three percent for 60 days is roughly 3 percent times six (the number of 60-day periods in a year), which lands near 18 percent per year, and that is on the full invoice rather than just the advanced portion, so the effective rate on the cash you actually received is higher still. A fee that looks like a rounding error is, in borrowing terms, comparable to or worse than a credit card. That is not an argument against factoring; it is an argument for using it only when the cash genuinely unlocks more value than the annualized cost, like fulfilling a new order you would otherwise lose.

## Recourse is the clause that bites

The other detail to read closely is whether the arrangement is recourse or non-recourse. Under recourse factoring, the cheaper and far more common kind, if your client never pays, you have to buy the invoice back. The financier was never really betting on your client's credit; they were lending against your obligation to make them whole. Non-recourse factoring shifts the default risk to the financier, but they price that risk in with a higher fee and tighter scrutiny of your client. The dangerous assumption is treating recourse factoring as if you have offloaded the risk. You have improved your timing, not your exposure, and a client who stiffs you leaves you owing the financier the advance plus the fee.

### Is invoice factoring the same as a loan?

Legally it is structured as a sale of a receivable rather than a loan, which is why the cost is called a discount or fee instead of interest, and why it can be available to a business that would not qualify for a bank loan. Economically, though, you should evaluate it like borrowing: money now in exchange for less money later, over a defined period. Converting the fee into an annualized rate is the only way to compare it fairly against an actual credit line, and doing so usually reveals factoring as the more expensive option you reach for when speed or access matters more than price.

### When does factoring actually make sense for a small business?

When the cash unlocks a return larger than the annualized cost, and when slow-paying clients are a structural feature of your business rather than a one-off. If accepting a big purchase order requires paying suppliers now but the client pays in 60 days, factoring that invoice to fulfil the order can be worth an 18 percent annualized cost if the order's margin clears it. Where it turns toxic is as a permanent crutch: factoring every invoice every month means you are permanently financing your operations at a double-digit rate, and that margin erosion compounds quietly until growth stops feeling profitable.
