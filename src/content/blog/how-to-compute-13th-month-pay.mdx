The 13th month pay is one of the few labor benefits that is genuinely universal: every rank-and-file employee gets it, regardless of position, status, or how their wage is computed, under [Presidential Decree 851](https://lawphil.net/statutes/presdecs/pd1975/pd_851_1975.html). It is not a bonus your employer chooses to give. It is a legal obligation, payable on or before December 24.

## The formula, and the word that trips everyone

The computation is total basic salary earned during the year, divided by twelve. Work a full year at a steady ₱25,000 and your 13th month pay is ₱25,000, clean and intuitive.

The complication hides in "basic salary," because it is narrower than people assume. It means your base pay and nothing else. Overtime pay, holiday premiums, night differential, and the various allowances that pad a payslip are all excluded from the computation. This is why someone who earns a healthy chunk of their take-home from overtime is sometimes disappointed by a 13th month that looks smaller than a typical month's gross; the benefit was never measuring gross. It was only ever measuring base.

<RegionalNote>
If you are paid the minimum wage, the "basic salary" feeding this formula is itself a regional figure. The daily minimum is set per region by the RTWPB wage orders, not nationally: as of mid-2026 it is roughly **₱695/day in NCR**, **₱600 in CALABARZON and most of Central Luzon**, **₱540/₱500 in Central Visayas (Cebu)**, and rising to **₱540 in Davao**. So the same rank-and-file job produces a noticeably larger 13th month in Manila than in Cebu, purely from the regional floor. The benefit itself and its ₱90,000 tax-free ceiling are national, but the base it is computed on is not: check your region's current wage order at [nwpc.dole.gov.ph](https://nwpc.dole.gov.ph/).
</RegionalNote>

The genuinely useful nuance is what happens to your basic salary when you are on unpaid leave or absent without pay. Because the formula sums what you *earned*, not what your monthly rate is, periods where you earned nothing pull the total down. A year with a long stretch of unpaid leave produces a 13th month below a full month's salary, and that is correct rather than a shortchange.

## When you did not work the whole year

Leave or get hired partway through the year and you are still entitled, on a prorated basis. The math just follows the months you actually worked. Eight months at ₱25,000 gives you (₱25,000 × 8) ÷ 12, which is ₱16,666.67. There is no minimum tenure to qualify; even a month of service earns its proportional slice.

## The tax most people forget

The 13th month pay is tax-exempt, but only up to ₱90,000, and that cap is shared with your other benefits and bonuses for the year. Cross it and the excess gets folded into your taxable income. This rarely bites a single-employer, mid-salary worker, but it routinely catches people who switched jobs mid-year, because the new employer's payroll has no idea what the previous one already paid out, and the two 13th-month amounts can quietly stack past the ceiling by year-end.
