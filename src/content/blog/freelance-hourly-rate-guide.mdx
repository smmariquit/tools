The most common way Filipino freelancers set their hourly rate is also the one that guarantees they earn less than they did as employees. They take their old monthly salary, divide by roughly 160 working hours, and quote that. It feels logical and it is wrong, because it silently assumes you bill every working hour, carry no business costs, and have an employer covering benefits you now pay yourself. Build the rate from the ground up instead and the number is meaningfully higher.

## The billable-hour illusion

A full-time month has around 160 working hours, but you will never bill all of them. Time goes to finding clients, sending proposals, invoicing, answering email, learning, and the gaps between contracts. A realistic billable ratio for a working solo freelancer is closer to 60%, so out of 160 hours you might actually invoice 90 to 100.

This single fact reshapes the math. If you need ₱60,000 a month to match your old take-home and you divide by 160, you quote about ₱375 an hour. But if only 100 of those hours are billable, that rate brings in ₱37,500, and you are ₱22,500 short every month. To genuinely clear ₱60,000 on 100 billable hours you need ₱600 an hour, not ₱375. The freelancers who burn out are usually the ones who priced for 160 billable hours and are now working 200 to make rent.

## Add the costs an employer used to absorb

As an employee, a lot was paid on your behalf and you never saw it. As a freelancer it is all yours. There is the employer share of SSS, PhilHealth, and Pag-IBIG that simply vanished, so you fund the whole contribution. There is no 13th month pay unless you save for it yourself, no paid leave, no sick days; an unworked day is an unearned day. Then there is real overhead: internet, electricity, a laptop that depreciates, software subscriptions, coworking fees, and the unpaid hours of running the business.

A sensible rate folds all of that in. Take your target take-home, add your monthly business costs, add a provision for the leave and 13th month you no longer get for free, and only then divide by your realistic billable hours. Skip any of those layers and you are subsidizing your clients out of your own pocket.

## The tax layer freelancers forget to price in

Here is the edge case that quietly erodes a rate that looked fine on paper: the BIR is not withholding anything for you. Every peso of income tax is yours to set aside. As a registered professional under the [TRAIN Law (RA 10963)](https://lawphil.net/statutes/repacts/ra2017/ra_10963_2017.html), you can elect the flat 8% on gross receipts above the ₱250,000 annual relief, or stay on the graduated brackets. Either way, the money has to come out of the rate you charge, not out of thin air.

Concretely, if you are on the 8% and you want ₱600 an hour to actually reach your pocket, you cannot quote ₱600, because 8% of it is going to the BIR each quarter. You quote enough that ₱600 survives the tax. Freelancers who price their rate as if it were net pay get a nasty surprise at the first quarterly deadline, then either eat the loss or scramble. The clean habit is to treat the tax as a cost of doing business and build it into the headline rate from day one.

<RegionalNote>
The BIR is not the only government office with an annual claim. Whatever city you register your practice in collects a **Local Business Tax** (the mayor's/business permit) at a rate it sets locally, renewed every January, separate from your income tax. If your profession requires a government licensure exam, you also owe a **Professional Tax Receipt (PTR)**, capped at ₱300 a year and due by January 31, paid to the province or city where you practice — government-employed professionals are exempt. These LGU costs belong in your rate too, and the exact figures vary by city, so confirm them with your city hall.
</RegionalNote>

### Should I charge hourly or per project?

Hourly protects you when scope is fuzzy or a client keeps changing direction, since every hour is paid. Per-project pricing rewards you for being fast and experienced, because you are paid for the outcome rather than the time. Many experienced freelancers quote per project but size the price using their hourly rate as the backstop, so a project that drags does not fall below their floor.

### How do international rates compare?

A common mistake is anchoring to the local market when your clients are abroad. If you serve overseas clients, you are competing in a global market where the rate ceiling is far higher, and pricing at Manila levels leaves a lot on the table. The flip side is the forex spread and platform fees, which shave a real percentage off foreign income before it lands in pesos. Price for the global rate, then account for what the conversion takes.

### What billable ratio should I assume?

If you are unsure, start at 60% and adjust from experience. New freelancers often sit lower because client acquisition eats their week; established ones with steady retainers can climb higher. The point is to assume less than 100%, because nobody bills every hour they work.
