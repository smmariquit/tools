If your PhilHealth deduction has crept upward over the past few years, that was the plan all along. The [Universal Health Care Act (RA 11223)](https://lawphil.net/statutes/repacts/ra2019/ra_11223_2019.html) scheduled a gradual climb in premiums, and 2026 sits at the top of that ramp: 5% of your basic monthly salary, the rate it was always heading toward.

## The computation, and why it is the easy part

The arithmetic itself is mercifully simple. Take 5% of your basic monthly salary, then split it down the middle, so you and your employer each shoulder 2.5%. Someone earning ₱30,000 has a total premium of ₱1,500, of which ₱750 comes out of their own pay. No brackets to look up, no rounding tables. If only the rest of payroll were this clean.

Where it gets interesting is the floor and the ceiling, because that is where the actual mistakes live.

The premium is computed on a salary of at least ₱10,000 and at most ₱100,000. The floor catches new hires more often than anything else. If you are on a ₱9,500 probationary rate, your premium is still figured on ₱10,000, not on what you actually earn, so your minimum total stays at ₱500 with ₱250 as your share. I have seen employers quietly miss this on probationary payroll and under-deduct by a few pesos a cutoff, which is the kind of tiny discrepancy that an auditor eventually surfaces. The ceiling does the opposite job at the top, capping the total premium at ₱5,000 no matter how far past ₱100,000 your salary runs.

One error worth watching for from the other direction: PhilHealth is computed on *basic* salary, not gross. If your employer is folding allowances into the base before applying the 5%, you are being over-deducted, and that is a fair thing to raise with HR.

## If you pay your own way

Freelancers, the self-employed, and OFWs are classified as direct contributors, which is the polite way of saying there is no employer to cover half. You shoulder the entire 5% yourself, against the same ₱10,000 floor, so the practical minimum is ₱500 a month. The coverage you get in return is identical to an employee's; you are simply paying both halves.
