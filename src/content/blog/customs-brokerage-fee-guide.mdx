People importing for the first time conflate two very different costs: the duties and taxes the government collects, and the professional fee the customs broker charges to clear the shipment. They are not the same, and the broker's fee is usually the smaller of the two. That fee follows a published schedule rather than the broker's mood, set by Customs Administrative Order No. 1-2001, and it scales with the dutiable value of your shipment. The whole clearance process now runs under the [Customs Modernization and Tariff Act (RA 10863)](https://lawphil.net/statutes/repacts/ra2016/ra_10863_2016.html), and only a licensed broker regulated by the [Customs Brokers Act (RA 9280)](https://lawphil.net/statutes/repacts/ra2004/ra_9280_2004.html) may file a formal entry on your behalf.

## The brokerage fee schedule by dutiable value

The professional fee for a formal entry steps up in brackets tied to your shipment's dutiable value, the CIF value of the goods that customs assesses duty against. These are the minimum fees under CAO 1-2001, the same figures the [brokerage fee calculator](/customs-brokerage-fee-calculator) uses.

| Dutiable Value (DV) | Minimum Brokerage Fee |
|---|---|
| Up to ₱10,000 | ₱1,300 |
| ₱10,001 to ₱20,000 | ₱2,000 |
| ₱20,001 to ₱30,000 | ₱2,700 |
| ₱30,001 to ₱40,000 | ₱3,300 |
| ₱40,001 to ₱50,000 | ₱3,600 |
| ₱50,001 to ₱60,000 | ₱4,000 |
| ₱60,001 to ₱100,000 | ₱4,700 |
| ₱100,001 to ₱200,000 | ₱5,300 |
| Over ₱200,000 | ₱5,300 + 0.125% of the excess over ₱200,000 |

The structure tells you something useful. Below ₱200,000 the fee is a flat amount per bracket, so a shipment valued at ₱60,001 and one valued at ₱100,000 cost the same ₱4,700 in brokerage. Above ₱200,000 it turns into a small percentage, 0.125 percent, on the excess, which keeps even a ₱5,000,000 shipment's brokerage fee modest relative to its value. The fee is described in CAO 1-2001 as a minimum, which matters: brokers may charge more for complex or hazardous cargo, but they should not quietly charge less and cut corners on the paperwork.

## The fee everyone forgets is not the brokerage fee

The genuinely important nuance is that the brokerage fee is a sliver of what you pay to release a shipment. The big numbers are the customs duty, computed from the tariff classification of your goods, and the 12 percent VAT levied on the duty-inclusive value. A ₱200,000 importation might carry a ₱5,300 brokerage fee but tens of thousands in duty and VAT combined. If you are budgeting an import on the brokerage fee alone, you are off by an order of magnitude. The broker's bill on top of the government's also includes arrastre, wharfage, and storage charges at the port, which accrue daily and punish slow paperwork.

The edge case worth knowing is the de minimis threshold. Under RA 10863, shipments with a value of ₱10,000 or below are exempt from duties and taxes entirely, which is why a small personal parcel often clears without a formal entry or a broker at all. The moment your shipment crosses that line, you are in formal-entry territory, you need a licensed broker, and the schedule above kicks in. Splitting a genuinely large order to dodge it is the kind of thing customs flags as undervaluation, so it is a threshold to respect rather than game.

## Exports are charged at half

If you are exporting rather than importing, the broker's professional fee for preparing the export declaration is 50 percent of the corresponding formal entry rate. The logic is that an export declaration is administratively lighter than clearing an inbound shipment through assessment and examination. So a shipment that would carry a ₱4,700 import brokerage fee is roughly ₱2,350 on the export side. The [calculator](/customs-brokerage-fee-calculator) has a toggle for this so you are not halving the figure by hand.

<RegionalNote>
The CAO 1-2001 schedule is national, but the broker behind it carries a local cost worth knowing about. A licensed customs broker, like any board-exam professional, must hold a current **Professional Tax Receipt (PTR)** — an LGU tax capped at ₱300 a year, due by January 31, paid to the province or city of practice (government-employed brokers are exempt). It does not change your clearance bill, but a legitimately practicing broker keeps this current, and the amount is set per LGU rather than nationally. Confirm specifics with the city where the broker is registered.
</RegionalNote>

### Is the brokerage fee the same as customs duty?

No, and conflating them is the most common budgeting mistake. The brokerage fee is the licensed broker's professional charge for filing your entry, set by CAO 1-2001. Duty and the 12 percent VAT are separate amounts the Bureau of Customs collects, and they are usually far larger than the brokerage fee.

### Do I always need a customs broker?

For a formal entry, yes, because under RA 9280 only a licensed customs broker may sign and file the declaration. Very low-value shipments at or below the ₱10,000 de minimis threshold can clear without a formal entry, which is why small personal parcels often arrive without you ever dealing with a broker.

### What is "dutiable value" and why does my fee depend on it?

Dutiable value is the basis customs uses to assess duty, generally the cost of the goods plus insurance and freight to the Philippines. The brokerage schedule is pegged to it because a higher-value shipment carries more responsibility and liability for the broker, not because the paperwork is proportionally heavier.
