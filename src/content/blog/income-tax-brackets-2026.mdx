Income tax in the Philippines confuses people for one specific reason: almost everyone assumes the rate applies to their whole salary. It does not. The brackets are marginal, which means each slice of your income is taxed at its own rate, and the first slice is free. Once that clicks, the rest of the table is just arithmetic.

The rates below come straight from the [TRAIN Law (RA 10963)](https://lawphil.net/statutes/repacts/ra2017/ra_10963_2017.html), which reset the schedule in 2018 and has not moved since.

## The 2026 graduated brackets

| Annual Taxable Income | Tax |
|---|---|
| ₱0 to ₱250,000 | None. Exempt. |
| ₱250,001 to ₱400,000 | 15% of the excess over ₱250,000 |
| ₱400,001 to ₱800,000 | ₱22,500 + 20% of the excess over ₱400,000 |
| ₱800,001 to ₱2,000,000 | ₱102,500 + 25% of the excess over ₱800,000 |
| ₱2,000,001 to ₱8,000,000 | ₱402,500 + 30% of the excess over ₱2,000,000 |
| Over ₱8,000,000 | ₱2,202,500 + 35% of the excess over ₱8,000,000 |

The number that does the most work here is the ₱250,000 exemption. Notice that it is annual *taxable* income, meaning after your SSS, PhilHealth, and Pag-IBIG contributions have already been subtracted. Spread over twelve months that exemption is about ₱20,833. A fresh graduate earning ₱18,000 a month is, after contributions, sitting comfortably below the line and paying no income tax at all, which genuinely surprises people who have been bracing for a deduction that never comes.

The thing to internalize is that crossing into a higher bracket does not retroactively tax your earlier pesos at the higher rate. If your taxable income is ₱260,000, only the last ₱10,000 is touched by the 15% rate. The ₱250,000 underneath it stays exempt. I mention this because the fear of a raise pushing you into a worse bracket is one of the most persistent myths in Filipino offices, and it is simply not how marginal brackets work.

## When the 8% flat rate is the smarter choice

If you are self-employed or a professional rather than a regular employee, the TRAIN Law gives you a second option: an 8% flat tax on your gross receipts, in place of both the graduated income tax and the separate percentage tax. It is only on the table if your annual gross stays under the ₱3,000,000 VAT threshold, and you have to formally elect it, usually on your first quarterly return for the year.

The decision comes down to expenses. The 8% rate is computed on gross receipts above the same ₱250,000 relief, with no deductions for your costs, so it rewards people whose work has very little overhead, a writer or a virtual assistant, for instance. If you run a business that burns real money on inventory, rent, or equipment, the graduated rates with itemized deductions will usually land you a lower bill, because you are taxed on profit rather than on everything that came in. There is a crossover point, and it depends entirely on your expense ratio, which is exactly the sort of thing worth modeling before you commit for the year.

The [income tax calculator](/income-tax-calculator) runs both paths and shows the bracket-by-bracket breakdown so you are not eyeballing the table. If a figure here ever drifts from what the BIR publishes, it means a regulation changed and I have not updated the math yet; the live schedule is always on [bir.gov.ph](https://www.bir.gov.ph/).
