In high school your average was kind: add up the grades, divide by the number of subjects, done. College quietly changes the rules, and the first time it bites is usually when a student computes a flattering simple average, then opens their portal and finds a colder number waiting. The difference is one word. College grades are weighted, so a 5-unit major counts for more than two and a half times what your 2-unit PE class does. I built the [GWA calculator](/gwa-calculator) because that single misunderstanding sends people into a panic every enrollment season, and the math underneath it is genuinely simple once you see it.

## The formula, and why units do the deciding

Your General Weighted Average is the sum of every grade multiplied by that subject's units, divided by the total units you carried. Take a normal semester: Math at 3 units with a grade of 1.50, Science at 5 units with a 2.00, and PE at 2 units with a 1.00. You multiply each pair (4.50, 10.00, 2.00), add them to get 16.50, then divide by the 10 total units for a GWA of 1.65. Notice what the weighting did. The 5-unit Science grade pulled the average toward itself far harder than the 2-unit PE did, which is exactly why a single bad grade in a heavy major can sink a semester that otherwise looked fine.

The edge case worth internalizing early is that not every unit on your study load necessarily counts toward the GWA your school uses for honors. Many universities exclude non-academic units like NSTP, and some treat PE separately, so the average printed on your transcript and the average your honors committee uses can legitimately differ. Before you celebrate or despair, check your own registrar's rule for which subjects are included, because computing the right number against the wrong rule set is the most common mistake I see.

## The trap of opposite-direction scales

There is no single national grading scale in the Philippines, and this is where students confuse themselves most. On the 1.00 to 5.00 system used by the University of the Philippines, PUP, and many state universities, 1.00 is the best possible mark and 3.00 is the passing line, so a lower GWA is the goal. On the 4.0 to 1.0 system common at Ateneo, De La Salle, and UST, the logic flips entirely: 4.0 is excellent and a higher number is better. A few schools still report straight percentages where 75 is the passing floor.

This matters more than it sounds. I have watched a transferee from a 4.0-scale school read their new 1.00-scale grades as catastrophic, when a 1.25 was actually near the top of the class. If you are moving between institutions or comparing yourself to a friend at another university, the raw GWA number is meaningless until you know which direction the scale runs. The [GWA calculator](/gwa-calculator) lets you switch scales so you can simulate exactly this.

## Converting your GWA for scholarships and study abroad

The same opposite-direction confusion gets much more expensive when you apply abroad. There is no single official conversion between a Philippine GWA and a foreign GPA. A UP-style 1.00 (where lower is best), an Ateneo or La Salle 4.0 QPI (where higher is best), and a straight percentage scale all describe achievement differently, and none of them maps cleanly onto, say, a US 4.0 GPA. Anyone who hands you one tidy "GWA-to-GPA" table is presenting their own opinion as if it were a standard, and it is not.

Here is who actually decides. Foreign graduate schools and scholarships almost always route your transcript through a **credential evaluator** such as [WES (World Education Services)](https://www.wes.org/), [ECE (Educational Credential Evaluators)](https://www.ece.org/), or IERF. Each runs its *own* methodology, and they explicitly require your **official transcript plus the issuing school's official grading scale and explanation** — for example, WES lists exactly which documents a Philippine applicant must send, and they have to come straight from your registrar, not from you ([WES required documents for the Philippines](https://applications.wes.org/required-documents/philippines/)). The evaluator then produces the GPA the program will actually trust.

So the practical advice is narrow and worth following. First, ask your registrar for an **official certification of the grading scale** (most Philippine registrars issue this on request, and it is the single document evaluators want most). Second, do not hard-convert your own GWA into a foreign GPA and present it as authoritative — even WES's own [iGPA calculator](https://applications.wes.org/igpa-calculator/) is an estimate that the formal evaluation can override. Third, read each program's stated requirement before you spend money: some accept a self-reported percentage, some demand a WES course-by-course report, and the difference decides which documents you chase. When you cite a conversion approach in your application, attribute it to the evaluator's methodology, never to a calculator you ran yourself.

## Why your honors GWA can differ from your transcript GWA

Beyond which subjects count, two more things quietly move the number. An INC (incomplete) usually does not enter the computation until it is completed, so a GWA that looks great mid-term can shift once that grade lands. And a subject you failed and retook is handled differently depending on the school: some replace the old mark, others average both attempts into your cumulative average forever. If you are chasing Latin Honors, that second rule is the one that decides whether one rough semester is recoverable or permanent.

### What grade do I need this semester to hit my target GWA?

Work backward from the total. Multiply your target GWA by your projected total units across your whole program to get the total grade-points you need, subtract the grade-points you have already earned, and what remains is what this semester must contribute. It is easier to let the [GWA calculator](/gwa-calculator) run that projection, but the logic is just the formula in reverse, and seeing it written out is what tells you whether your goal is still mathematically reachable.

### Does NSTP or PE lower my GWA?

It depends on your university. Where these carry units and are included, a low PE grade absolutely drags your average, just with less force than a major because of its smaller unit weight. Where the registrar excludes them from the honors computation, they will not touch the number that matters for graduation distinctions. Confirm your school's policy rather than assuming, because the two schools next to each other often disagree.
