Clients are often startled when an architect quotes a fee that runs into six figures for a house they have not built yet. The fee feels arbitrary until you know it is not a number the architect invented; it is anchored to a professional standard. The United Architects of the Philippines publishes Standards of Professional Practice, and SPP Document 202 covers the design services most people commission, computing the fee as a percentage of the project construction cost. The practice itself is regulated under the [Architecture Act of 2004 (RA 9266)](https://lawphil.net/statutes/repacts/ra2004/ra_9266_2004.html). I built the [architectural fee calculator](/architectural-fee-calculator) to apply the schedule, and this guide explains how it works and where the surprises hide.

## The fee is a percentage of construction cost

The most common method in SPP 202 is the percentage of project construction cost. The architect's basic fee is a percentage applied to the total cost of constructing the building, and that percentage varies with the project type and complexity, broadly landing in the single digits for ordinary buildings and higher for complex or specialized ones. A straightforward residence sits at one tier, a more complex structure like a hospital or a building with heavy technical systems sits higher because the design effort is genuinely greater. So a ₱5,000,000 house at a roughly 6 percent basic fee implies about ₱300,000 in architect's fees, and that figure scales directly with the construction budget, which is why the estimate moves the moment your scope grows.

## The detail clients miss: it is a minimum, and it is regressive

Two things about the schedule trip people up. First, the SPP percentages are stated as minimum basic fees, not ceilings. They represent the floor below which the standard says professional service cannot be sustainably rendered, so a quote at or slightly above the schedule is normal, and a quote far below it should make you ask what is being left out. Second, the percentage is regressive against project size: a larger construction cost usually carries a lower percentage, because the design effort does not scale one-for-one with budget. A ₱50,000,000 project does not pay ten times the fee of a ₱5,000,000 one at the same rate; the percentage tapers, so the absolute fee rises but proportionally less. Reading the schedule as a flat percentage on any project size overestimates large jobs and underestimates the floor on small ones.

## Reimbursable expenses are separate, and so are the other methods

The basic fee buys design and the standard scope of documents and construction-phase services. It does not absorb reimbursable expenses, which are billed on top: things like government permit fees, plan reproduction, travel for out-of-town projects, renderings and scale models, and the cost of specialist consultants the project requires. Clients who budget only the basic fee are sometimes surprised by these, even though the standard treats them as separate by design. SPP 202 also recognizes that percentage-of-cost is not always appropriate, and provides alternatives: a multiple of direct personnel expenses for hard-to-scope work, a per diem or hourly rate for consultation, and a fixed lump sum where the scope is genuinely fixed. The right method depends on how definable the work is, which is a conversation to have with the architect before signing.

<RegionalNote>
The fee and reimbursables are not the only recurring costs of practice. A licensed architect must carry a current **Professional Tax Receipt (PTR)** — an LGU tax on board-exam professionals, capped at ₱300 a year and due by January 31, paid to the province or city where they practice; government-employed architects are exempt, and once paid it lets them practice nationwide. The UAP SPP figures above are a national professional standard, but the PTR and any local business permit are set and collected per LGU. If you are vetting an architect, a current PTR is part of confirming they may legally sign your drawings — and the exact local fees vary, so check the city of practice.
</RegionalNote>

### Is the UAP fee schedule legally mandatory?

It is a professional standard rather than a price-control statute, so it functions as the recommended floor and the reference point the profession uses, not a rate the government enforces peso-for-peso. In practice it carries real weight: architects use it to justify their fees, and clients use it to sanity-check a quote. What RA 9266 does mandate is who may legally practice architecture and sign drawings, which is the more important protection for a client. You can negotiate around the schedule, but a quote that sits dramatically below it is usually a sign the scope of service has been quietly narrowed.

### What is not included in the basic architectural fee?

The basic fee covers the agreed design and documentation services, but reimbursable expenses fall outside it and are billed at cost. Expect permit and licensing fees, blueprint and document reproduction, perspective renderings or physical models if you ask for them, transportation for site visits beyond the agreed radius, and the fees of engineering or specialty consultants. Major revisions driven by client-side scope changes after a phase is approved are also typically billed as additional services rather than absorbed, so deciding clearly before each phase is finalized keeps the total predictable.
