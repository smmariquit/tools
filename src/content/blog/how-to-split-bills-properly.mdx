The fight is never about the money. It is about the ₱180. Someone orders a steak and two cocktails, someone else has rice and an iced tea, the bill gets divided by the headcount, and the iced-tea person quietly resents it for the rest of the night. Splitting evenly is the fastest method, not the fairest one, and most disputes come from using the fast method when the situation called for the fair one. ## The three honest ways to split, and when each one is fair

An even split divides the total by the number of people, and it is fine when everyone consumed roughly the same, a buffet or an unlimited-rice place where individual items are meaningless. An itemized split assigns each line to whoever ordered it, then shares only the truly common items like the platter everyone picked at. This is the fair default for a normal restaurant table. A proportional split divides by capacity rather than consumption, which is how housemates often handle rent and utilities, the person in the bigger room or higher salary carries more. The mistake is defaulting to even out of politeness when the table clearly ordered unevenly, because the quiet eater subsidises the heavy one every single time.

## The part everyone forgets: service charge and VAT

In a Philippine restaurant the menu price is rarely the price you pay. Most sit-down places add a 10 percent service charge and the 12 percent VAT is already baked into listed prices in compliant establishments, though some still print pre-VAT menus. The thing to get right is that the service charge applies to the whole bill, so it has to be split in the same proportion as the food, not tacked evenly onto an itemized split. Say four friends run up ₱4,000 in food but one person's share is ₱1,600. With a 10 percent service charge, that person owes ₱1,760, not ₱1,600 plus a flat ₱100. Splitting the service charge evenly quietly overcharges the light eaters again, which is the exact problem you were trying to avoid.

## Shared household bills are a different animal

Splitting a restaurant tab is a one-night settlement. Splitting a Meralco bill among housemates is a recurring negotiation, and the fair approach is usually a hybrid. The fixed portion of a utility, the part you pay just for being connected, splits evenly because everyone benefits from having power at all. The consumption portion is where proportional logic helps: if one housemate runs an aircon nightly and the others do not, an even split is a subsidy. A common, workable compromise is to split the base evenly and assign the aircon room an agreed surcharge, which you can ballpark with the [electric bill calculator](/electric-bill-calculator) before anyone feels cheated.

### How do you split a bill when one person did not drink or eat much?

Switch from an even split to an itemized one. Assign each person their own orders, share only the genuinely common items like an appetizer everyone touched, and then apply the service charge proportionally to each person's subtotal rather than as a flat per-head add-on. The iced-tea friend ends up paying for an iced tea plus their slice of the shared plate and their proportional service charge, which is a few tens of pesos rather than an equal quarter of someone else's cocktails.

### Should the tip be split evenly or by what each person ordered?

A discretionary tip on top of the service charge is the one item where an even split is defensible, because a tip rewards service to the table as a whole rather than to individual plates. That said, if the gap in orders is large, splitting the tip proportionally is more consistent with how you handled the rest of the bill. The only wrong answer is being inconsistent, itemizing the food meticulously and then dividing the tip evenly tends to reopen the same argument you just closed.
