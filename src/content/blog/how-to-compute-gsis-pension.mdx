If you spent your career in government, the first thing to unlearn is the SSS math. The GSIS pension runs on a completely different statute, the [Revised Government Service Insurance Act of 1997 (RA 8291)](https://lawphil.net/statutes/repacts/ra1997/ra_8291_1997.html), and it computes your Basic Monthly Pension from your compensation, not from a bracketed salary credit. People who switch between the private sector and government tend to assume the two systems behave alike. They do not, and the difference shows up most in the final number.

## The two pieces that build your Basic Monthly Pension

Your pension starts from the Average Monthly Compensation, the AMC, which is roughly the average of your salary over the last three years of service. GSIS then adds a flat ₱700 to that figure to get what the law calls the Revalued Average Monthly Compensation (the RAMC). That small ₱700 revaluation matters more than it looks for lower-paid employees, because it lifts the base that every percentage below is multiplied against.

The Basic Monthly Pension itself is 37.5% of your RAMC, plus another 2.5% of the RAMC for every year of service beyond the fifteenth. So someone with exactly 15 years gets 37.5%, and the percentage climbs from there. On an AMC of ₱40,000, the RAMC becomes ₱40,700, and a retiree with 30 years of service earns 37.5% plus fifteen extra years at 2.5% each, which is another 37.5%, for 75% of ₱40,700, or about ₱30,525 a month.

To even reach this monthly pension you generally need at least 15 years of service, be at least 60 years old, and be separated from service. Fall short of 15 years and GSIS pays a cash benefit instead of a lifetime pension, the same harsh cliff that catches under-contributed SSS members.

## The 90% cap that trims your top end

Here is the edge case practitioners watch for. The Basic Monthly Pension can never exceed 90% of your Average Monthly Compensation, and notice that the cap is measured against the AMC, not the revalued RAMC. Take that same ₱40,000 AMC, but now with 36 years of service. The formula would give you 37.5% plus 21 years at 2.5%, which is 90% of the RAMC, or about ₱36,630. The cap pulls you back to 90% of the plain ₱40,000, which is ₱36,000. The ₱700 revaluation you were counting on gets clipped off at the very top. Long-tenured retirees on a high salary are the ones who routinely hit this ceiling and wonder why their last few years of service added nothing.

## Lump sum or monthly: a real decision

GSIS lets you take the first five years of your pension as a discounted lump sum paid up front, after which the monthly pension resumes for life. The alternative is an immediate monthly pension with a smaller cash payment. Taking the five-year lump sum means no monthly cash flow at all during those five years, so it only makes sense if you have a concrete use for the capital, like clearing a loan, rather than letting it sit. I have seen retirees grab the lump sum out of habit and then scramble through five lean years. Run both paths against your actual expenses before deciding.

### Is the GSIS pension the same as the SSS pension?

No. They are separate funds under separate laws, and you cannot be a mandatory member of both for the same employment. GSIS covers government workers under RA 8291; SSS covers the private sector. If you worked in both sectors across your career, you may qualify for two separate pensions, each computed under its own rules, rather than one combined benefit.

### What happens to my service before I turned the qualifying age?

All your creditable years count toward the service total that drives the 2.5% multiplier, but the old-age pension itself only starts once you are separated and at least 60. Years keep adding value right up to retirement, which is why staying in service longer at a high salary is the most reliable way to lift the final pension, until the 90% cap catches you.
