Toll on the Philippine expressways is the kind of cost people underestimate until the trip is over and they tally the RFID deductions. A Manila-to-Baguio run can quietly eat several hundred pesos in toll alone before you have spent a single peso on fuel. The rates below are the schedules currently in force, approved by the Toll Regulatory Board under the Toll Operation Decree (Presidential Decree 1112), and I keep them in sync with the [toll calculator](/toll-calculator) so you can plan a multi-expressway route without flipping between operator websites.

One thing worth understanding before you read the tables: most of these expressways run a closed system, which means you take a ticket or tap in at entry and pay based on the distance between your entry and exit points. That is why the rate from Nichols to Calamba is not a flat number but the difference between two distance markers, and it is also why intermediate trips can be computed by subtraction.

## The RFID split is an ownership map, not a feature choice

The thing nobody explains clearly is why you may need two RFID tags. It is not a technical limitation, it is that the expressways are run by two competing operators and each only reads its own tag. Autosweep, operated by San Miguel Corporation, covers SLEX, the Skyway system, NAIAX, the STAR Tollway, MCX, and TPLEX. Easytrip, operated by Metro Pacific Tollways, covers NLEX, SCTEX, CAVITEX, CALAX, and the C5 Link. If your trip crosses from one operator's network to the other, as a Baguio run via NLEX and TPLEX does, you cannot rely on a single tag for the whole drive.

The practical edge case that ruins trips is an under-loaded tag rather than a missing one. A tag with insufficient balance does not warn you in advance; it simply fails at the gantry, holds up the lane, and on some expressways triggers a violation notice. Before a long drive I top up both tags well past what the route should cost, because the cost of a blocked lane and a penalty is far higher than carrying a buffer.

### Why do some expressways charge a flat rate while others vary by exit?

Open systems like CAVITEX charge a fixed toll at a barrier regardless of how far you go, while closed systems like SLEX and NLEX charge by the distance between your entry and exit. That is why a closed-system table lists a different number for every destination and an open system needs only one or two figures.

### How do I compute a trip between two middle exits on SLEX or NLEX?

Subtract the two rates measured from the base. A trip from Marilao to San Fernando on NLEX Class 1 is ₱180.00 minus ₱45.00, which is ₱135.00. The [toll calculator](/toll-calculator) does this automatically and can chain multiple expressways for a single trip.

### Do buses and trucks really pay double or triple?

Yes. Class 2 is generally twice the Class 1 rate and Class 3 roughly three times, because heavier vehicles cause disproportionately more wear on the pavement. You can see the exact multiple in every table above.

## What happens if your RFID isn't scanned at entry

A closed system computes your toll from the gap between entry and exit, so it needs a recorded entry tap. If the entry gantry never reads your tag — a dead sticker, a tag mounted where the reader can't see it, no beep, or a system lag that fails to transmit the entry — the exit plaza has no starting point to subtract from. Rather than waving you through for free, it defaults to the longest possible trip and charges the **full terminal-to-terminal toll** for that expressway. MPTC has confirmed this directly: when the entry information is not transmitted in time, the exit scanner "will charge you the farthest distance," which is why a short Bocaue hop can be deducted as though you had driven from the far end of NLEX.

This only happens on closed, distance-based expressways (SLEX, NLEX, Skyway Stage 3, STAR, TPLEX, CALAX, SCTEX). Open, flat-rate barriers like CAVITEX and NAIAX charge one fixed fee at the plaza no matter what, so there is no entry record to lose. The [toll calculator](/toll-calculator) above has an "entry wasn't scanned" checkbox on each closed-system leg that swaps the estimate to that maximum charge, so you can see the worst case before you dispute it.

### How do I dispute a maximum toll charged because my entry wasn't scanned?

The Toll Regulatory Board gives you 30 days from the transaction to report an incorrect charge to your RFID provider. Call Easytrip at 1-35000 or Autosweep at (02) 5318-8655, or email them, with your plate number, the date and time, and your actual entry and exit plazas. MPTC's Easytrip system also runs a backroom autocorrect that often reconciles the default charge a short while after you exit, so check your transaction history before assuming the deduction is final. Keep your dashcam clip or toll receipt if you have one — it speeds up the refund.

### How do I avoid being charged the full-length toll?

Watch the gantry as you enter: wait for the beep and the green light or the LED that shows your balance and the lane opening, and never tailgate through a closing barrier. Keep both tags loaded well past the trip cost, because a tag that fails for low balance at entry can leave you without a clean entry record. Mount the sticker where the reader expects it — on the windshield or headlight, not loose on the dashboard — and replace any tag that reads intermittently.
