Pag-IBIG is the deduction nobody complains about, partly because it is small and partly because almost everyone forgets it is there until they need a housing loan. The Home Development Mutual Fund operates under [RA 9679](https://lawphil.net/statutes/repacts/ra2009/ra_9679_2009.html), and after holding its computation base steady for decades, it finally moved it.

## The salary cap that doubled

For years, contributions were computed on a maximum of ₱5,000 in monthly compensation, which capped the employee deduction at a near-symbolic ₱100. That base has now doubled to ₱10,000. The rate did not change, so the practical effect is straightforward: at 2% of a ₱10,000 ceiling, the most that leaves your payslip is ₱200 a month, with your employer matching another ₱200 on top.

The rate has a small wrinkle at the very bottom. If you earn ₱1,500 or less in a month, your share is 1% while your employer still puts in 2%. Above ₱1,500, both sides contribute 2%, which describes essentially everyone with a regular job. So for practical purposes, anyone earning ₱10,000 or more is looking at ₱200 out, ₱200 matched, ₱400 saved every month.

That extra ₱100 a month compared to the old cap is not really a loss. It lands in your Pag-IBIG Regular Savings, which earns tax-free dividends that have historically sat somewhere around 6%, and it quietly builds the contribution history that determines how large a housing loan you can eventually take out. Of the three mandatory deductions, this is the one most clearly working as savings rather than as a shared fund you may never personally draw on.

## Paying voluntarily

If you are self-employed, an OFW, or a voluntary member, the matching employer share simply is not there, so you carry both halves yourself. That works out to 4% of your declared monthly income, capped the same way, for a maximum of ₱400 a month. Many voluntary members choose to pay the full ₱400 precisely because it is the cheapest way to keep their savings and loan eligibility growing.

For anyone willing to go further, the same fund runs the MP2 program, a separate five-year savings vehicle with consistently higher dividends, which I cover in the [MP2 guide](/pagibig-mp2-calculator).

The [Pag-IBIG calculator](/pagibig-calculator) shows your exact deduction and what it does to your net pay. Rates are published on [pagibigfund.gov.ph](https://www.pagibigfund.gov.ph/); if mine ever drift from theirs, the official figure wins and I would appreciate the heads-up.
